## A City, Not a Tribe

The Prophet ﷺ had built a mosque. He had established the Brotherhood. But the community of Medina was more complex than the Muslim community alone.

There were the Ansar — Aws and Khazraj, Muslim now, but with a history of violence between them that was only months past. There were the muhajirin — arriving from Mecca, integrating into a city they had never lived in. There were Arab tribes of Medina who were allies of one group or another, some Muslim, some not. And there were the Jewish tribes — Banu Qaynuqa, Banu al-Nadir, Banu Qurayza, and others — with their own internal divisions, their own relationships with the Arab tribes, their own religious identity and communal institutions.

This was not a tribe. It was a city. And a city required something a tribe had never needed: a governing document.

## The Charter

The document that scholars have called the Constitution of Medina — known in the Arabic sources as *Sahifat al-Madinah* — was drafted by the Prophet ﷺ in the early months after the Hijrah.

It was a written document. This itself was significant — in a culture of oral agreements and tribal custom, a written charter was a formal, binding, and permanent commitment in a way that a verbal agreement was not.

It addressed, clause by clause, the relationships between all the groups of Medina.

The opening established the foundation: *"This is a document from Muhammad the Prophet, governing relations between the believers and Muslims of Quraysh and Yathrib, and those who follow them and are attached to them and who crusade along with them."*

What followed was a detailed legal framework — approximately forty-seven clauses in the form that scholars have reconstructed — covering defense, financial obligations, dispute resolution, the treatment of enemies and allies, and the specific rights of the Jewish tribes.

## One Ummah

The document declared what the Brotherhood had already established in social terms: the Muslims — muhajirin and Ansar together — formed one ummah.

Not one tribe. Not two tribes who had agreed to cooperate. One community, defined by faith, with mutual obligations that superseded the tribal loyalties each had come from.

*"The believers shall not leave a debtor among them without giving him assistance for ransoms or blood money."*

*"The believers shall be against whoever among them rebels or seeks to spread injustice or sin or enmity or corruption among the believers — they shall be against him as one, even if he be the son of one of them."*

The last clause is the most radical: even if the wrongdoer is your own son, the ummah stands against the wrong. The community's commitment to justice superseded even blood loyalty — the most powerful loyalty in Arabian culture.

## The Jewish Tribes

The constitution then addressed the Jewish tribes with a specificity that makes its pluralistic intent clear.

*"The Jews of Banu Awf are one community with the believers — the Jews have their religion and the Muslims have theirs."*

This clause, repeated with variations for each Jewish tribe named in the document, established the foundational principle: different religious communities could coexist within one political community. The Jews did not have to become Muslims. They retained their religion, their communal institutions, their internal governance.

What they agreed to was civic: they would bear their share of the costs of defending Medina. They would not support Quraysh or other enemies against the community. They would submit disputes with the Muslim community to the Prophet ﷺ for resolution.

Within these civic obligations, their religious and communal autonomy was preserved.

*"And that between them — mutual sincere advice, and consultation, and righteous dealing, and no betrayal."*

The document was not a conversion charter. It was a social contract — defining the terms of coexistence between communities that would continue to differ in their fundamental beliefs while sharing a city, a civic responsibility, and a framework of mutual obligation.

## The Arbiter

At the center of the document was the Prophet ﷺ — not as a king in the traditional sense, but as an arbiter.

*"Whenever you differ about anything, it is to be referred to Allah and to Muhammad."*

This clause defined the Prophet's ﷺ role: disputes that could not be resolved within communities, disputes between communities, questions about the interpretation of the charter's obligations — these came to him. His judgment was binding.

This was an authority derived not from force but from consent — each party to the charter had agreed to this mechanism of dispute resolution. It gave the Prophet ﷺ an authority over the diverse communities of Medina that his position as a tribal chief of any one group could never have provided.

## What the Constitution Built

Within a few months of the Hijrah, the Prophet ﷺ had established:

A mosque — the physical and spiritual center of the community. A Brotherhood — the social fabric that embedded the refugees within the host community. And a Constitution — the political framework that organized the diverse groups of Medina into a functioning political entity.

These three acts together were the foundation of the Medinan state — perhaps the most consequential political construction in human history, given what it eventually produced.

The mosque established that the community was defined by worship. The Brotherhood established that the community's bonds transcended blood. The Constitution established that governance required negotiated frameworks of rights and responsibilities — and that a just political order could encompass diversity without requiring uniformity.

The Prophet ﷺ had arrived in Medina as a refugee with a hundred-camel price on his head. Within months, he was the recognized arbiter of a city that included multiple tribes, multiple religions, and the most complex social landscape in Arabia.

Not through force. Through wisdom. Through the mosque built with his own hands, the brothers declared in his presence, and the document that defined how they would all live together.

The world he was building was unlike anything Arabia had seen. And Arabia was only the beginning.
