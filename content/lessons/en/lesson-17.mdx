## A Line Preserved Across Twenty Generations

When we left Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> in Section 1, he was a young man in the valley of Mecca — son of Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, builder of the Kaaba, carrier of the prophetic covenant in the Arabian branch of the family.

Between Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Muhammad ﷺ lie approximately twenty generations. Twenty generations across which something extraordinary happened — not a dramatic miracle, not a sudden intervention, but a quiet, deliberate, divine preservation of a line.

The Prophet ﷺ described it himself:

> *"Allah chose Kinanah from the sons of Ismail, and chose Quraysh from Kinanah, and chose Banu Hashim from Quraysh, and chose me from Banu Hashim."*
> — Sahih Muslim, 2276

Step by step. Generation by generation. Narrowing, refining, selecting — until the line arrived at one man.

## Why Lineage Matters

Before we trace this line, we need to understand why it matters at all.

Prophethood is not inherited. It is not a birthright that passes from father to son like a title of nobility. Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was chosen by Allah — not because of who his father was, but despite the fact that his father carved idols. Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was raised in a palace, but his lineage as an Israelite gave him nothing until Allah chose him.

So why does the lineage of Muhammad ﷺ matter?

Because Prophethood is a mission — and a mission requires a platform. When a man stands up and makes the greatest claim any human being can make, the first question his audience asks is: *who are you?*

If the answer is *nobody* — an outsider, a man of low birth, someone with no standing or credibility — the message faces an immediate barrier that has nothing to do with its truth. The Arabs of Mecca, in particular, lived by a code of honor deeply tied to ancestry. A man without lineage was a man without a voice.

Allah, in His wisdom, ensured that this barrier did not exist. When Muhammad ﷺ stood up to speak, he stood as a member of the most respected tribe in Arabia, from its most honored clan. His lineage was not his qualification — but it was his platform.

## From Ismail عليه السلام to Adnan

The descendants of Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> grew and spread across Arabia. They intermarried with the Arab tribes around them — Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> himself married from the tribe of Jurhum — and their children became the ancestors of the northern Arab tribes.

The genealogical record from Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> to Adnan — an ancestor of the Prophet ﷺ approximately ten generations before him — is considered reliable and documented. Beyond Adnan back to Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, the chain exists but scholars of hadith advise caution about its precise details.

What matters is the confirmed, unbroken line from Adnan forward — through Maad, Nizar, Mudar, Ilyas, Mudrikah, Khuzaymah, Kinanah, an-Nadr, Malik, Fihr — and it is here that a name appears that every Muslim knows.

Fihr. Also called *Quraysh*.

## Quraysh — The Tribe of the Kaaba

The tribe of Quraysh took its name from Fihr ibn Malik, their ancestor — though some scholars attribute the name to Qusayy, who reunified and reorganized the tribe several generations later. What is beyond dispute is what Quraysh represented in Arabia.

They were the custodians of the Kaaba.

Since the time of Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, the Kaaba had been the spiritual center of the Arabian Peninsula. Every year, tribes from across Arabia made pilgrimage to Mecca. Every year, they passed through Quraysh territory, depended on Quraysh hospitality, and conducted commerce under Quraysh authority.

This custodianship gave Quraysh something no military victory could buy: *prestige*. Their word carried across tribal lines. Their decisions about the Kaaba were respected by Arabs who might otherwise have nothing to do with them.

Allah mentions this distinction in the Quran:

The winter and summer trading caravans of Quraysh moved safely across Arabia — no small thing in a land where tribal raiding was a constant. Their connection to the Kaaba was their protection and their power.

## Qusayy — The Reunifier

A few generations before the Prophet ﷺ, the fortunes of Quraysh were gathered and consolidated by a remarkable man named Qusayy ibn Kilab.

Qusayy had grown up away from Mecca, raised by his mother in the land of the tribe of Quda'ah after she remarried. When he returned as a young man and discovered his Qurayshi lineage, he worked — through negotiation, alliance, and ultimately force — to reclaim Mecca and reunite the scattered clans of Quraysh within it.

He established the key institutions of Mecca: the *Dar al-Nadwa* — the assembly house where tribal decisions were made; the *Siqayah* — the provision of water for pilgrims; the *Rifadah* — the feeding of pilgrims; and the *Liwa* — the right to bear the war standard.

He was, in effect, the architect of Mecca as a functioning city-state. And his descendants inherited these positions of honor — passing them, generation by generation, down toward the man who would one day stand on the hill of Safa and call the entire city to Tawhid.

From Qusayy came Abd Manaf. From Abd Manaf came Hashim. And Hashim gave his name to the clan that would become the most honored in all of Arabia.

The Banu Hashim. The family of the Prophet ﷺ.
