## After the Worst, the Greatest

Ta'if had been the lowest point. Bleeding feet, rejection, stones, an offer of divine revenge that he had refused.

He returned to Mecca. He had to seek formal protection from a Meccan leader just to enter his own city — Mutim ibn Adi agreed to extend his protection, and the Prophet ﷺ entered Mecca under the shelter of a man who was not a Muslim.

The city that had been his home for forty years required the favor of one of its leaders for him to walk its streets without being attacked.

And then — in the midst of this, the lowest personal and political moment of the entire Makkan period — Allah took His Prophet ﷺ on a journey.

## The Buraq and the Night

One night — the scholars differ on the exact date, but place it in the period after the Year of Grief — Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> came to the Prophet ﷺ at the Masjid al-Haram.

He brought with him an animal called the Buraq — described as white, larger than a donkey and smaller than a mule, whose stride reached the limit of its sight. The Prophet ﷺ mounted it, and they traveled — in a single night — from Mecca to Jerusalem.

<Quran surah="17" ayah="1" />

*Exalted is He who took His servant by night from al-Masjid al-Haram to al-Masjid al-Aqsa, whose surroundings We have blessed, to show him of Our signs. Indeed, He is the Hearing, the Seeing.*

At al-Masjid al-Aqsa — the furthest mosque, in Jerusalem — the Prophet ﷺ was met by the assembled Prophets. He led them all in prayer. The final Prophet, leading every Prophet who had come before him. The completion of the chain, standing at its head for two rak'ahs at the site that had been the direction of prayer for centuries.

## Through the Seven Heavens

From Jerusalem, the ascent began.

Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> took the Prophet ﷺ upward — through the levels of the heavens, each one opening at Jibril's knock, each one asking who had come and whether he had been sent for.

*He has been sent for.*

*Then welcome — what a blessed arrival.*

In the first heaven: Adam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the father of humanity, looking to his right at souls and smiling, looking to his left at souls and weeping. The righteous and the damned, all his children.

In the second heaven: Yahya and Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — cousins by blood, Prophets by divine appointment, greeting their brother.

In the third heaven: Yusuf <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — given half of all beauty, radiating something that the Prophet ﷺ could barely describe.

In the fourth heaven: Idris <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the one Allah had raised to a high station.

In the fifth heaven: Harun <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the brother of Musa, the eloquent one.

In the sixth heaven: Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — who wept when the Prophet ﷺ passed. Asked why, he said: *I weep because a young man sent after me will have more of his nation enter Jannah than mine.*

In the seventh heaven: Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the father of Prophethood, leaning against al-Bayt al-Ma'mur, the Frequented House of the heavens, circumambulated by seventy thousand angels every day — angels who will not return to it until the Day of Judgement, so vast is the number of angels.

## The Sidrat al-Muntaha

Beyond the seventh heaven, the Prophet ﷺ reached the Sidrat al-Muntaha — the Lote Tree of the Utmost Boundary. The place where the knowledge of creation ends and what lies beyond is known only to Allah.

<Quran surah="53" ayah="13-18" />

*And he certainly saw him in another descent — at the Sidrat al-Muntaha — near it is the Garden of Refuge. When there covered the Lote Tree that which covered it, the sight of the Prophet neither turned aside nor overstepped the limit. He certainly saw of the greatest signs of his Lord.*

Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — who had accompanied him through every stage of the journey — stopped here. He told the Prophet ﷺ that this was the limit he could reach. Beyond it, the Prophet ﷺ continued alone.

What happened beyond the Sidrat al-Muntaha is described in the tradition with a reverence for its limits. The Prophet ﷺ was brought near to Allah — in a manner that befits Allah's majesty, without comparison to anything in creation.

And there, at the utmost limit of the created world, the command was given.

*Fifty prayers. Daily. For your community.*

## Musa and the Fifty Prayers

The Prophet ﷺ descended from the Sidrat al-Muntaha carrying the command of fifty daily prayers.

He passed Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> in the sixth heaven. Musa asked what he had been commanded.

*Fifty prayers.*

Musa's response was immediate, rooted in his own painful experience of leading a difficult community: *"Go back to your Lord and ask for a reduction. Your community cannot bear fifty prayers."*

The Prophet ﷺ — perhaps from the shyness he always felt before Allah — hesitated. Musa insisted.

He went back. The prayers were reduced by ten. He came down. Musa sent him back again.

This continued — back and forth, each time a reduction, each time Musa saying: *go back, your community cannot bear it* — until the prayers were five.

*"Go back and ask for further reduction,"* Musa said.

*"I am too shy before my Lord to keep returning,"* the Prophet ﷺ said. *"I am satisfied and I submit."*

A voice called: *The decree has been passed. Five prayers, with the reward of fifty. My word does not change.*

Five in count. Fifty in reward. The only pillar of Islam received not on earth through an angel, but in the highest place of the created world, directly.

## Morning in Mecca

He returned to Mecca before morning.

He told people what had happened.

Quraysh erupted. A night journey to Jerusalem and back — impossible. A journey through the heavens — madness. They ran to Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> with the gleeful certainty of people who believed they had finally found the thing that would break even his closest companion's faith.

*"Your companion claims he went to Jerusalem and back in one night. What do you say?"*

Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> asked: *"Did he say that?"*

*"Yes."*

*"Then I believe him. I believe him about something greater than this — I believe him about revelation from heaven."*

Al-Siddiq. The Confirmer of Truth. The title came from this moment — from a man who understood that the scale of a claim is irrelevant when the source of the claim has already been established as trustworthy.

Some among the Muslims who had been weak in their faith left it. The Quran acknowledged this directly — calling the Night Journey a *fitnah*, a trial, for people:

<Quran surah="17" ayah="60" />

And the Prophet ﷺ — challenged to describe al-Masjid al-Aqsa, which he had visited only that night — described it with a precision that the Meccans who had been there could not fault.

## The Gift in the Darkness

The timing of the Isra and Mi'raj was not coincidental.

He had lost Khadijah رضي الله عنها. He had lost Abu Talib. He had been stoned in Ta'if. He had returned to Mecca requiring the borrowed protection of a non-Muslim to walk its streets.

And then Allah took him — personally, directly, in a night journey unlike anything any Prophet before him had experienced — through Jerusalem and through the heavens, to the utmost limit of created reality, to a nearness with Allah that cannot be described in the language of the created world.

The message was clear: the support you have lost was human support. What I give you is not human.

You stood in Jerusalem and led every Prophet who ever lived in prayer. You ascended past every level of heaven. You were brought to a nearness that Jibril himself could not follow. You were given the gift of prayer — not through an intermediary but directly.

You are not abandoned. You have never been abandoned.

The darkest period of the mission was followed by the highest point any human being has ever reached. Not as a compensation for the suffering — but as a reminder of what the suffering was in the context of.

The Isra and Mi'raj did not end the difficulties of the Makkan period. But it reoriented everything. A man who had been to the Sidrat al-Muntaha and returned walked through Mecca differently than before.

He was still waiting for the door to open. But he knew, now, with a knowledge that went beyond faith into direct experience — what was on the other side of every closed door.

Allah.
