## When Everything Else Failed

Quraysh had tried mockery. It had not stopped the message from spreading.

They had tried torture. The companions رضي الله عنهم had borne it with a steadfastness that had impressed even some of their tormentors.

They had tried diplomacy — sending men to Abyssinia to have the emigrants returned. The Negus had refused.

They had tried negotiation — coming to Abu Talib with offers of wealth and status in exchange for withdrawing his protection. He had refused.

And through all of it, the community of believers had grown — quietly, steadily, in the face of everything Quraysh threw at it.

Quraysh decided to try something different. Not the focused cruelty of individual persecutors against individual victims, but a collective, systematic, total isolation.

They would starve them out.

## The Document in the Kaaba

The leaders of Quraysh gathered. They drafted a document — a formal, signed pledge — and they hung it inside the Kaaba as a solemn covenant.

The terms were total: no Meccan was to sell food or any goods to Banu Hashim or Banu Muttalib. No one was to buy from them. No one was to marry into their families or give their daughters to them in marriage. No one was to conduct any social or commercial relations with them — no visiting, no sitting with them, no negotiations of any kind.

The only exit from the boycott was to hand Muhammad ﷺ over to Quraysh for them to deal with as they chose.

The Prophet ﷺ, Khadijah رضي الله عنها, the believers, and all of Banu Hashim — including Abu Talib, who was not a Muslim but refused to abandon his nephew — withdrew to a valley outside Mecca called Shi'b Abi Talib.

Three years. In the valley.

## The Sound of Children Crying

The narrations do not soften what the three years were like.

The community ran out of food. They ate leaves. They ate leather — boiling it until it was soft enough to chew, not for nutrition but to suppress the physical sensation of starvation. The sound of children crying from hunger carried from the valley into Mecca — and some of the Meccans who heard it were troubled by what they heard, even if they did nothing about it yet.

Khadijah رضي الله عنها spent the last of her considerable wealth. She had been one of the wealthiest women in Mecca — what remained of that wealth after years of spending on the Muslim community went into the valley with them, and was consumed by three years of siege.

Abu Talib — elderly, bearing the full weight of his clan's situation and the constant political pressure of Quraysh's demands — aged visibly during these years. He was a man who had spent his life navigating the complex dynamics of Meccan tribal politics, but this was a sustained assault that left no room for the maneuvering he had always relied on.

The Prophet ﷺ bore it with his companions رضي الله عنهم, sharing what there was, leading the prayers in the valley, teaching, sustaining.

## Conscience in the Night

Not all of Mecca was comfortable with what was being done.

Hisham ibn Amr was one of the men whose conscience would not let him sleep through it. At night — in the dark, when no one would see — he would load a camel with food and drive it to the entrance of the valley. He would strike its side, send it in, and leave quickly before anyone noticed.

He was not alone, but he was among the few.

During the sacred months — the months in which the normal conventions of Arabia required that conflict be suspended — there was slightly more room for movement. Some trade was possible. But even this relief was incomplete and inconsistent.

The narrations capture what sustained the community through these years: not political maneuvering, not strategy, but the quality of the bond that had been formed between the Prophet ﷺ and his companions رضي الله عنهم — and the unshakeable conviction that what they had accepted was true and that the One who had told them it was true had not forgotten them.

## The Worm and the Parchment

In the third year, the Prophet ﷺ came to his uncle Abu Talib with a piece of information that would have seemed impossible to verify.

*"Allah has sent woodworm against the document of Quraysh. It has eaten everything in it except the name of Allah."*

Abu Talib went to the assembly of Quraysh and presented this claim with the directness of a man who had nothing to lose.

*"My nephew tells me that Allah has sent woodworm to eat your document. If he is right — end your injustice. If he is wrong — I will hand him over to you."*

The leaders of Quraysh went to the Kaaba. They took down the parchment that had hung there for three years as a symbol of their collective power against a small community of believers.

They opened it.

The parchment had been eaten — eaten completely — except for the opening phrase: *In Your name, O Allah.*

The supernatural confirmation was precisely what several men who had already felt the weight of the injustice needed. Mutim ibn Adi — a man who was not a Muslim but had always found the boycott excessive — stood and declared it over. He was joined by others. The boycott collapsed.

Banu Hashim and the Muslim community came out of the valley.

## The Cost of Three Years

The valley had ended. The siege had broken. The document was eaten.

But the three years had taken something that could not be returned.

Khadijah رضي الله عنها was physically spent. The woman who had been the wealthiest woman in Mecca, who had been the Prophet's ﷺ partner and his first refuge and his most constant support — had given everything, and the giving had cost her body more than it could afford.

Abu Talib was broken in health. The years of sustained pressure, the weight of holding together a clan under total economic siege while managing the political assault of all of Quraysh, had done what decades of ordinary life had not.

Both of them would be dead within the year.

The boycott had failed in its purpose — it had not stopped Islam, had not broken the community, had not produced the surrender of Muhammad ﷺ. But it had done something else: it had cost the Prophet ﷺ the two people who had been most personally present in his life through the years of his mission.

Their deaths were coming. And the year they died together would be remembered, in the Seerah, by a name that says everything about what it felt like from the inside.

*Am al-Huzn.* The Year of Grief.
