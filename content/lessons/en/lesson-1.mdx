## A Word Before We Begin

Before we can understand the life of Prophet Muhammad ﷺ — his struggles, his patience, his mission — we need to understand what a Prophet actually is.

Not the word. The reality.

Because the word *"Prophet"* is used so casually today that its weight has been lost. So let us start here, at the very beginning.

## A Human Being, Chosen by Allah

A Prophet is not an angel. He is not a god. He is not a mythological figure beyond the reach of ordinary understanding.

A Prophet is a human being — a man who eats, sleeps, feels pain, loves his family, and walks among his people — whom Allah, the Most High, selects from among all of creation to receive His revelation and carry it to humanity.

This selection is not earned. It is not a reward for worship or scholarship. It is a pure gift from Allah, a divine appointment that no amount of effort or piety can secure.

Allah says:

<Quran surah="6" ayah="124" />

The Prophets were the best of people in character, in lineage, in soundness of mind and heart — not because perfection earns prophethood, but because Allah chose those who were most fit to carry the heaviest trust ever placed on human shoulders.

## What Makes a Prophet Different?

Every Prophet shared certain qualities that distinguished him from all other human beings.

**First: Truthfulness (As-Sidq).** A Prophet never lies — not before prophethood, not after. His entire life is an open record. History itself becomes his witness.

**Second: Trustworthiness (Al-Amanah).** What Allah revealed, he conveyed completely and faithfully — adding nothing, removing nothing, softening nothing out of fear or personal interest.

**Third: Intelligence and Wisdom (Al-Fatanah).** The task of a Prophet — calling people away from everything they have known and loved, facing ridicule, persecution, and disbelief — demands extraordinary wisdom. Every Prophet possessed it.

**Fourth: Conveying the Message (At-Tabligh).** A Prophet hides nothing of what Allah commanded him to deliver. Even when the message was unpopular, even when it endangered his life, he stood and spoke.

**Fifth: Protection from Error in the Message (Al-Ismah).** Allah protected His Prophets from falling into error in what they conveyed. The message arrived to humanity pure and intact.

These are not qualities they claimed for themselves. These are qualities that history, their companions, and even their enemies were forced to acknowledge.

## They Were Still Human

This is perhaps the most important thing to hold in your mind.

The Prophets were not superhuman. They were not removed from the struggles of life. They knew hunger and grief. They buried their children. They were mocked, chased from their homes, and threatened with death.

Prophet Ayyub (Job) <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> endured years of illness and loss. Prophet Musa (Moses) <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> knew the fear of a fugitive fleeing across a desert. Prophet Ibrahim (Abraham) <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was thrown into a fire. Prophet Yunus (Jonah) <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> cried out to Allah from the darkness inside a whale.

And our Prophet, Muhammad ﷺ, buried his children, lost his closest companions, was driven from his homeland, and stood alone for years against an entire civilization.

This humanity is not a weakness in the story. It is the point of the story.

Because it means that everything they demonstrated — the patience, the trust in Allah, the unshakeable certainty — is something a human being is actually capable of. And that is the most profound argument they could ever make.

## The Word: Nabi and Rasul

In Arabic, there are two words used for those chosen by Allah to deliver His message.

**Nabi** (نبي) — from the root meaning *news* or *tidings*. A Nabi receives revelation from Allah.

**Rasul** (رسول) — meaning *messenger*. A Rasul is a Nabi who is additionally sent to a specific people with a specific scripture or law.

Every Rasul is a Nabi, but not every Nabi is a Rasul. The distinction matters in theology, but for our journey through the Seerah, what matters most is this: they were all part of one unbroken chain, carrying one essential message, from the first human being to walk this earth to the last Prophet who would ever be sent.

And that chain — its beginning, its purpose, and its seal — is exactly what we are about to explore.
