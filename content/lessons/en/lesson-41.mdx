## Two Pillars Removed

The boycott had ended. The community came out of the valley. There should have been relief — and perhaps there was, briefly.

But the valley had taken its toll on two people in particular, and the world was about to show the Prophet ﷺ what it felt like to lose both of them within weeks of each other.

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid. Twenty-five years of marriage. The first believer. The woman who had covered him on the worst night of his life and told him, with the certainty of someone who knew him completely, that Allah would never disgrace him.

Abu Talib ibn Abd al-Muttalib. The uncle who had held his hand when he was eight years old and walked him through a world that no longer had his grandfather in it. The man who had faced the entire power of Quraysh and said: by Allah, I will never hand you over.

They were sick. And the illness that had settled on them in the valley was not lifting.

## Khadijah — The First to Go

Khadijah رضي الله عنها died in Ramadan of the tenth year of prophethood. She had given everything — her wealth, her health, her years — to the mission that had begun in her own home on the night her husband had come trembling from a cave.

The Prophet ﷺ sat with her through her final days. He was beside her when she died.

He buried her himself in Jannat al-Mu'alla, the cemetery of Mecca.

There is a narration that captures something of what her death meant. Before she died, Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had come to the Prophet ﷺ with greetings for her from Allah — and the promise of a house of pearl in Jannah, in which there would be no noise and no fatigue.

The honor from Allah confirmed what she had been in the story of Islam. But it did not make the empty place beside him any less empty.

He grieved. Openly. With the full grief of a man who had loved completely and lost completely.

<Quran surah="93" ayah="8" />

*And He found you poor and enriched you.*

She had been the enrichment. And now she was gone.

## Abu Talib — The Deathbed

Abu Talib had been ill since the valley. The sustained pressure of the boycott years — managing a clan under total siege while holding off the political demands of all of Quraysh — had broken his health beyond what the end of the boycott could repair.

The Prophet ﷺ was at his deathbed. The narrations preserve the scene with a precision that speaks to how deeply it was felt.

He leaned close to his uncle and said:

*"O uncle — say La ilaha ill Allah. A single word by which I can intercede for you before Allah."*

Abu Talib opened his eyes. He looked at his nephew — the boy he had held by the hand when he was eight, the young man he had taken on a caravan to Syria, the adult whose mission he had protected for the last ten years at the cost of everything.

Abu Jahl and Abdullah ibn Abi Umayyah were in the room, and they spoke: *"O Abu Talib — will you turn away from the religion of Abd al-Muttalib?"*

Abu Talib said — and these were among his last words: *"I am on the religion of Abd al-Muttalib."*

He died without the shahada.

The Prophet ﷺ wept. He said: *"I will ask forgiveness for you as long as I am not forbidden from doing so."*

The permission was then withheld — revelation came:

<Quran surah="9" ayah="113" />

*It is not for the Prophet and those who have believed to ask forgiveness for the polytheists, even if they were relatives.*

He wept again when he heard this. He wept for Abu Talib with the grief of a nephew who had loved his uncle — and the grief of a Prophet who had wanted for his uncle what his uncle had not chosen.

## The World After

With Abu Talib's death, the tribal protection that had kept Quraysh from physical assault on the Prophet ﷺ was gone.

Abu Lahab briefly assumed leadership of Banu Hashim. Then, almost immediately, he withdrew the clan's protection from his nephew. A man without tribal protection in Arabia was legally vulnerable to any harm.

The persecution that followed was immediate and personal. Men began to throw dirt on him in the street. During his prayer, filth was thrown on him. He could not walk through Mecca without harassment of a kind that had been politically prevented while Abu Talib lived.

He was now, in the tribal law of the Jahiliyyah, nobody. The grandson of Abdul-Muttalib, the man Mecca had called Al-Amin for forty years, the Prophet of Allah — was walking through his own city with no protection and no recourse.

He sought refuge with other tribes. He approached the tribe of Banu Amir, Banu Kilab, Banu Kalb, and others — asking for their protection so he could continue his mission. Each one refused.

He went to Ta'if. That story comes next. And it would be, in his own words, the worst day of his life.

## What Grief and Mission Look Like Together

*Am al-Huzn* — the Year of Grief — is not a chapter in the Seerah that the tradition dwells on with self-pity. It is recorded with honesty, in its full weight, and then the narrative continues.

Because that is what the Prophet ﷺ did. He grieved. He wept for Khadijah رضي الله عنها for the rest of his life. He wept at Abu Talib's deathbed and again when the verse came forbidding intercession for him. He experienced the full human reality of loss — not the loss of distant figures, but the two people who had been most personally present in his life through the most difficult years of his mission.

And then he continued.

Not because the grief passed quickly. Not because he was less than human in his emotional experience. But because the mission was not conditional on the presence of any human support. He had always known, from the morning he came trembling down the mountain with five verses, that what he was carrying was from Allah and returned to Allah — and that no human pillar, however beloved, was its actual foundation.

The year that stripped away his last human shields was the year that showed, most clearly, what the foundation actually was.

And the foundation held.
