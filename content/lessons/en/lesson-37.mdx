## The Hunter Comes Home

Hamzah ibn Abdul-Muttalib <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> was the Prophet's ﷺ uncle — though they were close in age, more brothers than uncle and nephew in the way they had grown up together. He was known across Arabia for two things: his courage in battle and his skill in the hunt. He would go out alone after lions. He would return with what he went after.

On the day that changed everything for him, he was returning from one of these hunts — bow in hand, the satisfaction of a successful trip still on him — when a slave woman intercepted him near the hill of Safa.

She told him what had happened while he was gone.

Abu Jahl ibn Hisham — one of the most powerful and most bitter opponents of Islam — had found the Prophet ﷺ near al-Safa and subjected him to a torrent of public insult and humiliation. He had struck him. He had walked away satisfied, having demonstrated to all who witnessed it that Muhammad ﷺ could be treated this way without consequence.

Hamzah رضي الله عنه listened. Something shifted in him that had nothing to do yet with theology.

## Honor Answers First

He went directly to the mosque. Abu Jahl was sitting with a group of men from Quraysh — comfortably, as a man sits when he believes he has won the day.

Hamzah رضي الله عنه walked up to him and struck him across the head with his bow.

*"Will you insult him when I follow his religion? Strike me back if you can!"*

The men around Abu Jahl moved to intervene. Abu Jahl stopped them. He knew what he had done — he knew it had been wrong by Arab standards — and he was not prepared to turn a manageable situation into a full tribal confrontation with Banu Hashim.

Hamzah رضي الله عنه walked out.

And then he was alone with what he had said.

*I follow his religion.*

He had said it in the heat of honor. He had said it to defend his nephew. He had said it without having thought through every implication. Now he thought through them.

He sat with it through the night. He examined what he knew about his nephew. He considered what he had heard of the message. He looked at his own life — what he valued, what he believed, what he could not in good conscience stand against.

By morning, the decision that had begun as tribal honor had become something far deeper. He went to the Prophet ﷺ and said: *I bear witness that you are the Messenger of Allah.*

The conversion that had started from a bow strike had ended in genuine faith.

## What His Entry Meant

The entry of Hamzah رضي الله عنه into Islam sent a signal through Mecca that everyone understood without it being explicitly stated.

Hamzah was not a man you physically threatened without calculating the cost very carefully. He was known as one of the most dangerous fighters in Arabia — the kind of man who hunted alone for sport what other men organized groups to face. His entry into the Muslim community meant that the option of simply beating individual Muslims in the street had become considerably more complicated.

The Prophet ﷺ was able to move through Mecca with a new degree of physical security. The most aggressive forms of direct intimidation became less frequent. Quraysh did not stop opposing Islam — they escalated in other ways — but the specific tool of unchecked physical violence against the Prophet ﷺ himself was now less available.

And for the Muslim community — the early believers who had been enduring years of persecution — the entry of Hamzah رضي الله عنه was something else entirely: it was proof that the message was reaching even those who seemed most deeply embedded in the Jahiliyyah's values. A warrior, a hunter, a man of Arab honor in its most complete form — and he had said yes.

## The Lion of Allah

The Prophet ﷺ gave him the title *Asadullah* — the Lion of Allah.

It was not a title given to flatter. It was a recognition of what Hamzah رضي الله عنه was and what he brought to the service of Islam. His courage was not manufactured by faith — it had existed before faith and been redirected by it. The same man who had hunted lions in the hills around Mecca would later charge into battle lines that others hesitated before, fighting with a ferocity that the enemies of Islam found terrifying and the companions رضي الله عنهم found inspiring.

The Prophet ﷺ also gave him a second title — *Sayyid al-Shuhada*, the Master of the Martyrs.

That title waited. It would only become fully understood years later, on a hillside called Uhud, where Hamzah رضي الله عنه would fight until his last breath and fall in a way that broke the Prophet's ﷺ heart open in grief.

But that is a later chapter. In the years of Mecca, Hamzah رضي الله عنه was something the persecuted community desperately needed: a sign that the walls of opposition were not as solid as they appeared. And a lion who now walked beside the Prophet ﷺ through the streets of the city that had been trying to break him.
