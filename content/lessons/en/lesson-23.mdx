## Monday Morning, the 12th of Rabi al-Awwal

In the city of Mecca, in a modest house in the neighborhood of Banu Hashim, in the same year that Allah's birds had scattered Abraha's army of elephants into the dust — a woman named Aminah bint Wahb gave birth.

It was a Monday morning. The child was a boy. She named him Muhammad.

The name means *the praised one* — from the same root as Ahmad, the name Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had used when he foretold his coming. A name that no Arab family had thought to use before. A name that had waited, through all the centuries of human history, for this particular child.

## The Signs at His Birth

The narrations of the Seerah record several signs that accompanied the night of his birth — and it is worth approaching them with the understanding that a tradition this early and this well-attested deserves serious consideration, not casual dismissal.

Aminah herself narrated that at the moment of his birth, a great light emerged from her that illuminated the palaces of Syria — a vision she had been given during her pregnancy now made real.

The same night, in Persia, the sacred fire of the Zoroastrians — said to have burned continuously for a thousand years — went out. In the palace of the Persian emperor, fourteen balconies of the royal hall collapsed. A lake in the region of Sawa, considered sacred, dried up overnight.

These are not Islamic embellishments. They are details found in the earliest Seerah sources, narrated through chains that scholars have examined. Whether one accepts every detail or not, their significance in the pattern of the story is clear: the arrival of the final Prophet ﷺ was not a quiet, local event. Its reverberations were felt, on the night of his birth, at the farthest edges of the civilized world.

## In His Grandfather's Arms

When the news reached Abdul-Muttalib, he came immediately.

He took the infant in his arms and carried him to the Kaaba. He walked around the House — the house he had spent his life protecting, the house whose Lord he had trusted completely in the most terrifying moment of his life — and he made dua for this child.

He named him Muhammad. When the people of Mecca asked why he had not given him one of the established names of their family, Abdul-Muttalib said simply: *I wanted Allah and all of creation to praise him.*

The name was unusual. It was also, as those who came to know its bearer would discover, the most fitting name ever given to a human being.

## Halimah's Blessing

Following the custom of Mecca's noble families, Aminah sought a wet nurse from the desert tribes to take her son for the first years of his life.

Women from the tribe of Banu Sa'd came to Mecca seeking infants to nurse. They passed by the infant Muhammad ﷺ and hesitated. He was an orphan. His father was dead. There was no guarantee of good payment.

Each woman passed him by and took another child.

One woman was left with nothing — Halimah bint Abi Dhuayb al-Sa'diyyah. She had found no child. Rather than return to her tribe empty-handed, she took the orphan.

The moment she lifted him and put him to her breast, something changed.

Her milk — which had been barely sufficient — flooded. Her she-camel, which had given nothing for days, filled its udders. Her donkey, which had been slow and tired on the journey to Mecca, moved swiftly on the return. And when she reached her tribe's camp, the animals of her neighbors gave their milk as normal — but hers gave abundantly.

Her husband said to her that night: *Halimah, by Allah — you have taken a blessed soul.*

<Quran surah="93" ayah="6" />

*Did He not find you an orphan and shelter you?*

The sheltering began here. In a Bedouin tent in the desert of Banu Sa'd, with a woman who had taken him when no one else would.

## The Splitting of the Chest

He stayed with Halimah for approximately four years — some narrations say five. He grew in the clean air of the desert, learned the most eloquent Arabic spoken anywhere on the peninsula, and played among the children of the tribe.

Then came the event that frightened Halimah so completely that she brought him back to Mecca.

He was with the other children when two men in white appeared. They laid him on the ground, opened his chest, removed from his heart something described as a black clot — the Quran uses the metaphor elsewhere of what gathers in the heart from sin and darkness — and they washed his heart with snow from a golden basin. Then they sealed him and departed.

The other children ran in terror to Halimah. *Muhammad has been killed!*

She ran to him and found him standing, pale, his face changed but unharmed.

He described what had happened. The narration reached us through him directly. Halimah and her husband decided immediately to return him to Mecca — not from rejection, but from fear of what they did not understand and could not protect him from.

The Quran references this event obliquely:

<Quran surah="94" ayah="1" />

*Did We not expand your chest for you?*

It was not a metaphor. It was a literal preparation — the heart of the man who would carry the Quran was prepared before he was old enough to read a word.

## The Losses That Came

Aminah received her son back and kept him close. He was approximately six years old when she decided to take him north to Yathrib — to visit the grave of his father Abdullah, and to spend time with the Banu Najjar, the family of his great-grandmother Salma.

They traveled north, the young Muhammad ﷺ and his mother. They spent a month in Yathrib. He played with his cousins, visited his father's grave, and began to know the city that would one day bear his name.

On the return journey, Aminah fell ill.

She died at a place called al-Abwa, between Mecca and Yathrib, far from home. The Prophet ﷺ — six years old — was with her when she died. His wet nurse Umm Ayman <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span>, who had accompanied them, took his hand and brought him back to Mecca.

His grandfather Abdul-Muttalib took him in with the tenderness of a man who knew exactly the weight of what this child carried, and the weight of what he had just lost.

For two years, the old man and the boy were inseparable. Abdul-Muttalib kept him close, gave him a place of honor at his side in the gatherings of Quraysh, and loved him with a love that everyone around them could see.

Then Abdul-Muttalib died. The boy was eight.

He went to live with his uncle, Abu Talib — his father's brother, a man of honor and loyalty who would be his protector for the next forty years without ever becoming his believer.

## The Shape of a Life Before the Mission

By the time Muhammad ﷺ was eight years old, he had:

Lost his father before birth. Lost his mother at six. Lost his grandfather at eight.

Been carried to the Kaaba in his grandfather's arms as a newborn. Been raised in the desert among the most eloquent Arabic speakers on earth. Had his heart prepared by angels in an event that frightened away the family who loved him. Traveled to the city of his father's grave and watched his mother die on the road home.

He had not yet spoken a word of prophethood. He had not yet retreated to the cave of Hira. He had not yet received a single verse of the Quran.

But the shape of the man — his capacity for patience, his reliance on no one but Allah, his freedom from the tribal pride and material dependency that defined his peers — was already there. Carved by loss. Expanded by angels. Sustained by a Lord who had sheltered him at every step.

<Quran surah="93" ayah="6-8" />

*Did He not find you an orphan and shelter you? Did He not find you lost and guide you? Did He not find you poor and enrich you?*

Yes. To all three. And the man who had been sheltered, guided, and enriched was going to spend the rest of his life returning that gift — passing it on, in full, to every soul that the darkness of the Jahiliyyah had reached and left hollow.

The foundation was laid. The character was formed. The heart was prepared.

Now, the world would wait a little longer — through a youth of extraordinary integrity, a young manhood of commerce and reputation, and a middle age of deepening contemplation — before the night that changed everything.
