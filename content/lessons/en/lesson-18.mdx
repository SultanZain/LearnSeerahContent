## A Name Earned, Not Given

His birth name was Amr. But Arabia did not remember him as Amr.

A year of severe famine struck Mecca and the surrounding lands. The pilgrims who came to the Kaaba arrived hungry. The people of Mecca were struggling. And Amr ibn Abd Manaf — great-great-grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ — did something that the people would not forget.

He slaughtered his camels. He boiled the meat into great cauldrons of broth. He crumbled bread into the broth with his own hands — *hashama al-khubz*, crumbled the bread — and fed everyone who came. Pilgrim and resident, rich and poor, known and stranger.

They called him *Hashim* after that. And the name that began as a description of an act of generosity became the name of a clan — the most honored clan in the most respected tribe in all of Arabia.

## The Architect of Mecca's Prosperity

Hashim was not only generous — he was visionary.

He looked at Mecca's position at the crossroads of ancient trade routes and understood what it could become. He traveled to the courts of the Byzantine Emperor in Syria and the rulers of Yemen, negotiating safe passage and trading agreements for the merchants of Quraysh.

The result was the establishment of the two great annual caravans — the winter journey south to Yemen and the summer journey north to Syria and beyond — that would define Mecca's economic life for generations.

## The Marriage in Yathrib

Hashim was known across Arabia — not just in Mecca. His trading journeys took him far, and his reputation preceded him everywhere.

On one of his journeys to Yathrib — the city that would centuries later be renamed Medina — he saw a woman of striking presence and independence: Salma bint Amr, of the tribe of Banu Najjar.

She was not a woman who accepted suitors easily. She had been married before and had made it a condition of any marriage that she would remain in her own city, under her own authority. Lesser men had found this unacceptable.

Hashim agreed to her terms.

They married. And from this union — between the most generous man of Quraysh and a noblewoman of Yathrib — was born a boy who would grow up in Yathrib before being brought to Mecca to claim his father's legacy.

They named him Shaybah. The world would know him as Abdul-Muttalib.

## The Son Who Was Smuggled Home

Hashim died on a trading journey to Gaza before his son Shaybah was old enough to know him. The boy grew up in Yathrib with his mother's family — unaware, for years, of the full weight of what he had inherited.

It was his uncle, Al-Muttalib ibn Abd Manaf, who eventually came to Yathrib to bring his nephew home. When Al-Muttalib rode into Mecca with the boy behind him on his camel, the people of Mecca assumed the child was his slave.

*"This is Abd al-Muttalib,"* they said — the slave of al-Muttalib.

His uncle laughed and corrected them: *This is my nephew, the son of Hashim, come to claim his father's place.*

But the name stuck. Shaybah ibn Hashim became Abdul-Muttalib — and the name that began as a misunderstanding became the name by which the grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ would be known to all of history.

## What Banu Hashim Carried

Abdul-Muttalib grew into a man who more than honored the legacy of his father. He became the leader of Mecca, the guardian of the Kaaba, the man who rediscovered the well of Zamzam — and the grandfather who held the infant Muhammad ﷺ in his arms and carried him to the Kaaba on the day of his birth.

But before we arrive at that moment, we need to understand the full picture of what Banu Hashim represented in Mecca.

In a city of ambitious clans, all competing for prestige and influence, Banu Hashim stood apart — not because they were the most powerful militarily, or the wealthiest commercially, but because of something harder to quantify and impossible to manufacture: *character*.

Generation after generation — Hashim, Al-Muttalib, Abdul-Muttalib — the clan was known for feeding the poor, honoring the guest, and carrying themselves with a dignity that even their rivals were forced to acknowledge.
