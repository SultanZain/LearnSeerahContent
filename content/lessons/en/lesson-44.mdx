## The City That Was Ready

Mecca had been the wrong city.

Not wrong in terms of divine plan — every stage of the Makkan period had its purpose, building a community of real believers tested and refined in fire. But wrong in the sense that its structure — the economic and social power tied to the Kaaba, the tribal hierarchies, the investment of the most powerful families in the existing order — made it constitutionally resistant to the message.

Yathrib was different.

Not because its people were better than the Meccans. But because what they had been through had left them in exactly the state that made them able to hear something the Meccans were too invested to hear.

## Two Tribes and a Devastating War

The Aws and Khazraj were the two main Arab tribes of the city they shared — a city of date palm gardens and small fortresses, more agricultural than commercial, tucked in the volcanic landscape of the Hijaz about four hundred kilometers north of Mecca.

Their relationship had been one of the bloodiest in Arabia. Generation after generation of conflict — raids, retaliations, betrayals, alliances formed and broken — had culminated in the Battle of Bu'ath, a particularly devastating engagement that had left both tribes exhausted in a way that went beyond the physical.

The strongest leaders of both tribes had died in the years around Bu'ath. The social structures that had sustained the conflict were depleted. The city that had spent generations at war with itself had arrived at a moment of genuine exhaustion — not the exhaustion that simply waits to fight again, but the exhaustion that wonders whether there might be another way.

Into this city, over the years before the Hijrah, the message of Islam would arrive. And the people who had destroyed themselves fighting each other would become the Ansar — the Helpers — the community that made the Prophet's ﷺ mission possible when his own city had tried to make it impossible.

## The Seed Planted by the Jews

There was something else in Yathrib that Mecca did not have.

The Jewish tribes of the city — Banu Qaynuqa, Banu al-Nadir, and Banu Qurayza — had lived alongside the Aws and Khazraj for generations. And in their disputes and their conversations, they had said something that had lodged in the Arab tribes' awareness.

*A Prophet is coming. We know of him from our scripture. When he comes, we will follow him, and with him we will destroy you.*

The Arab tribes had heard this as a threat. But the statement carried within it something they had no framework to process — the idea of a coming Prophet, announced in an existing scripture, whose arrival was expected by people who claimed knowledge of the divine.

When six men of the Khazraj sat with the Prophet ﷺ at al-Aqabah during the pilgrimage season and heard his message, the recognition was immediate. They said to each other:

*"By Allah — this is the Prophet that the Jews have been threatening us with. Let us be the first to reach him."*

What the Jewish tribes had intended as a threat had become, unintentionally, a preparation. The Aws and Khazraj had spent years carrying an expectation they could not quite name — and when they heard the message at al-Aqabah, something clicked into place.

## Six Men at al-Aqabah

The pilgrimage season brought tribes from across Arabia to Mecca. The sacred months made the roads safe and the city open to everyone. And the Prophet ﷺ had been using these seasons — since the Year of Grief had left him without tribal protection in his own city — to present his message to visiting delegations.

Most had refused. Some politely. Some rudely. The tribes had their calculations and their existing alliances and their reluctance to take on the political cost of associating with a man whom the most powerful tribe in Arabia had officially rejected.

But at the pass of al-Aqabah — a meeting point on the road above Mecca — he encountered six men of the Khazraj.

He sat with them. He recited the Quran. He explained the message.

They accepted. All six of them, in that meeting, on that day.

They returned to Yathrib carrying something that would change the city — not a set of rules, not a political alliance, but the recognition that what they had been waiting for had arrived. They told their families. They told their tribe. They told the men of the Aws. They told anyone who would listen.

And Yathrib began to change.

## Musab ibn Umayr — The First Teacher

The following year, a larger group returned to Mecca for the pilgrimage — twelve men this time, representing both the Aws and the Khazraj, both tribes present for the first time together in the context of Islam.

They met the Prophet ﷺ again at al-Aqabah. They took a pledge — the first pledge of Aqabah — committing to the fundamental principles of the faith.

And they asked for a teacher. Someone who could come with them to Yathrib and teach the Quran, explain the religion, and establish the community properly.

The Prophet ﷺ sent Musab ibn Umayr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>.

Musab was everything his mission required. Young — probably in his early twenties. From one of the wealthiest families in Mecca, which meant he understood how to move in the company of leaders. Beautiful in manner and appearance. And utterly transformed by Islam — he had given up silk and luxury and his family's wealth for a message that his family had tried to beat and starve out of him.

He arrived in Yathrib and began teaching.

His effectiveness was extraordinary. He would sit with people, recite the Quran, explain what it meant, answer questions. One by one and then family by family, the people of Yathrib were accepting Islam.

The breakthrough came when Sa'd ibn Mu'adh <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> — the chief of the Aws tribe, one of the most influential men in the entire city — accepted Islam. The narration preserves the moment: a man named Sa'd ibn Ubadah directed Musab to him, saying that if Sa'd accepts, everyone behind him will accept. Musab sat with Sa'd and recited. Sa'd accepted. And as Sa'd ibn Ubadah had predicted, his entire clan followed.

## A City Transformed

By the time the next pilgrimage season arrived — the year that would become the year of the Hijrah — the situation in Yathrib was entirely different from what it had been two years before.

There was now a substantial Muslim community in the city. Both tribes — Aws and Khazraj, who had spent generations trying to destroy each other — were united within it. The Quran was being recited in the homes of the date palm gardens. The prayer was being established. A community was forming.

And they had sent a message to the Prophet ﷺ: come. We will receive you. We will protect you as we protect our own families.

The city that had been called Yathrib would, in time, be renamed. It would become known as al-Madinah — the City. The City of the Prophet ﷺ. The place where the mission that had been born in Mecca would find the soil it needed to grow into something the world had never seen.

But before that could happen, there was one more meeting at al-Aqabah. The largest, the most secret, and the most consequential.

The second pledge. The night covenant. The moment the Hijrah became real.
