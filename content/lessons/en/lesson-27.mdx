## The Caravan Prepares to Leave

Abu Talib was a man of honor and limited means. As the head of Banu Hashim after his father's death, he carried the family's dignity but not its wealth — Hashim's commercial empire had not passed to him in full, and providing for a large family on a trader's income required the seasonal caravans to Syria.

The summer caravan was preparing. The men of Quraysh loaded their goods onto camels. The city buzzed with the organized chaos of departure.

And Muhammad ﷺ — twelve years old, who had already lost more than most men lose in a lifetime — looked at his uncle preparing to leave and could not let him go.

He clung to him. The narrations describe a boy who simply would not release his uncle's hand.

Abu Talib looked at the child. He thought of Abdullah. He thought of Aminah. He thought of Abdul-Muttalib, who had loved this boy more than anything and had left Abu Talib with the silent charge of continuing that love.

*"By Allah,"* he said, *"I will take him with me, and we will never be separated."*

He lifted his nephew onto the caravan. And they headed north.

## The Road to Syria

The trading road from Mecca to Syria was ancient — one of the routes that Hashim himself had established generations before. It passed through the Hijaz, through the outskirts of the Hejazi highlands, north through the settled lands of the Levant, until it reached the trading centers of Syria.

The caravan moved through the landscape that the boy had never seen — beyond the familiar hills of Mecca, beyond the territories he knew, into a wider world.

He would have seen, for the first time, the remains of ancient civilizations — the ruins of peoples that the Quran would later describe as warnings. He would have passed through lands that carried the histories of Prophets he had not yet been told he was connected to.

The road was also a school. Trade required judgment — knowing when to buy and when to sell, how to read a market, how to negotiate without losing face, how to honor an agreement when circumstances made honoring it costly. Abu Talib was teaching his nephew a profession. He was also teaching him what it meant to be a man of his word in a world where words were the primary currency of trust.

## The Monk Who Was Waiting

Near the city of Busra, in what is now southern Syria, there was a monastery. And in that monastery lived a monk named Bahira — known by his title rather than his birth name, a Christian scholar who had inherited from a long line of monks before him a collection of ancient texts.

Those texts described a Prophet who was to come.

Caravans passed by the monastery regularly — the trading routes of Arabia passed through this region seasonally. Bahira had watched many caravans in his years at the monastery. He had never invited one in.

This time he did.

He had seen something as the caravan from Mecca approached. A cloud — moving against the direction of other clouds — following the caravan and shading it from the sun. And as they stopped to rest near a tree, he saw the branches of the tree bend down toward one particular figure in the group, extending their shade as if deliberately.

He sent a messenger to the caravan: come, all of you, to eat with me.

The Meccans, surprised at the invitation from a monk who had never extended one before, accepted.

## The Examination

Bahira looked at the group carefully as they ate. He had expected to find what he was looking for among the older men — the leaders, the merchants of standing. He did not find it there.

He asked: *"Is there anyone among you who did not come to the meal?"*

The men said: *Only a boy — a young one — whom we left to watch the baggage.*

*"Bring him,"* Bahira said.

The boy was brought. And Bahira looked at him with the attention of a man who had spent decades preparing for this moment.

He asked him questions — about his sleep, his dreams, his daily habits. He looked at his physical characteristics — his build, his bearing, the features that the texts described. And then he asked to see his back, between his shoulders.

There it was. The Khatam al-Nubuwwah — the Seal of Prophethood. A raised mark, described in the ancient texts, between his shoulder blades.

Bahira took Abu Talib aside.

*"What is your relation to this boy?"*

Abu Talib said: *He is my son.*

*"He is not your son,"* Bahira said. *"His father cannot be living."*

*"He is my brother's son,"* Abu Talib corrected himself.

*"What happened to his father?"*

*"He died before the boy was born."*

Bahira nodded. It matched. Everything matched.

## The Warning

Bahira looked at Abu Talib with the urgency of a man who understood something that the man before him did not yet fully grasp.

*"Take your nephew home,"* he said. *"And guard him carefully. Great things are ahead of this boy. If those who know what I know were to see him and recognize what I recognize — they would seek to harm him. This boy has a tremendous future."*

He spoke specifically of dangers. He told Abu Talib to be vigilant, to be protective, to not allow the boy to continue into the heart of Syria where more eyes might fall on him.

Abu Talib listened. He took his nephew, completed his trading business more quickly than planned, and returned to Mecca.

## What Bahira Knew

The encounter with Bahira raises a question worth sitting with: how did a Christian monk in Syria know what the people of Mecca did not?

The answer is in his inheritance — the texts passed down through generations of monks, texts that described a coming Prophet with enough specificity that Bahira could recognize the signs when he saw them.

This is itself a confirmation of what the Quran states directly: the coming of Muhammad ﷺ was foretold in the earlier scriptures. Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had announced him by name. The Torah and Injeel, in their original forms, contained descriptions that people like Bahira had spent their lives studying.

<Quran surah="7" ayah="157" />

*Those who follow the Messenger, the unlettered Prophet, whom they find written in what they have of the Torah and the Gospel.*

Bahira was not the only one who knew. He was simply the one who acted on what he knew — who reached out across the dining table of a monastery in Syria to a twelve-year-old boy from Mecca, and said, in his way: *I see you.*

The boy returned to Mecca. He went back to his hills and his flock. He continued to be the young man that his community knew — honest, quiet, reliable.

But somewhere in Syria, an old monk sat in his monastery knowing that the world had passed by his door on its way to becoming something entirely new.

And in Mecca, a twelve-year-old boy went back to watching sheep, carrying a mark between his shoulders that he could not yet read — and a future that only Allah and a few careful observers could see.
