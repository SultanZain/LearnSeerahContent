## Sixty Miles Alone

He left Mecca on foot.

He had tried the tribes around Mecca — Banu Amir, Banu Kilab, Banu Kalb — each one turning him away with the polite refusal of people calculating their interests and finding a rejected man with no tribal protection to be a liability.

Ta'if was sixty miles away through the rocky terrain of the Hijaz. He walked it with only Zayd ibn Harithah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> beside him — his freed slave, his companion, the man who had chosen to stay with him when he could have returned to his family years before.

The city of Ta'if sat in a cooler, greener elevation than Mecca — a city of orchards and vineyards, wealthier than it appeared from a distance. It was the home of the tribe of Thaqif, one of the significant powers of western Arabia, and of the idol al-Lat — one of the three great goddesses of the Arabian peninsula, whose shrine drew pilgrims from across the region.

He arrived. He sought a meeting with the three leaders of the city.

## What the Leaders Said

The three brothers who led Thaqif received the Prophet ﷺ. He sat with them and presented his message — the same message he had brought to Mecca, to the tribes, to everyone he had approached. The oneness of Allah. The reality of the Day of Judgement. The invitation to follow.

They heard him. And then they answered.

The first said: *"If you are truly the messenger of Allah, then you are too great for me to speak with. And if you are lying about Allah, then I should not speak with you either."*

The second said: *"Could Allah not find anyone better than you to send?"*

The third said: *"By Allah, I will never speak to you. If you are a Prophet, it would be too dangerous to reject you. And if you are lying about Allah, you are not fit to be spoken to."*

They rose and left. And then they did something worse than refuse him.

They sent their slaves and the street children of Ta'if to follow him as he walked away — throwing stones, shouting mockery and abuse, driving him out of the city with a cruelty that was more than rejection. It was designed to humiliate.

Zayd <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> walked beside him and in front of him — placing his own body between the Prophet ﷺ and the stones, taking blows meant for the man he had chosen to serve. His head was cut. The Prophet's ﷺ feet bled.

They drove him out of the city.

## The Garden and the Dua

He found a garden on the outskirts of Ta'if — owned by Utbah and Shaybah, sons of Rabia, men of Quraysh who happened to have property in the area. He sat beneath a grapevine, away from the city that had just stoned him, and he made a dua.

It is one of the most preserved and most beloved duas in the Islamic tradition. Not because of its eloquence — though it is beautiful. But because of what it reveals about the state of the man who made it.

> *"O Allah — I complain to You of my weakness, my lack of resources, and my humiliation before the people. O Most Merciful of the merciful — You are the Lord of the weak, and You are my Lord. To whom do You consign me? To a distant stranger who will mistreat me? Or to an enemy who has been given power over me? If You are not angry with me, I do not care what I face — but Your grace is more expansive for me. I seek refuge in the light of Your face, by which all darkness is illuminated and the affairs of this world and the next are set right — that Your anger should befall me or Your wrath descend upon me. I have only to please You until You are pleased. There is no power and no might except through You."*

He was bleeding, alone, rejected by the last place he had gone seeking support. And he was talking to Allah with the directness and the vulnerability of a man who had nothing left to hide behind and nowhere left to turn.

This is tawakkul in its most complete form — not the tawakkul of a man who has options and chooses to trust Allah anyway, but the tawakkul of a man who has no options and knows that the One to whom he is speaking is enough.

## A Grape and a Christian Slave

Utbah ibn Rabia — one of the garden's owners, a man of Quraysh who had not accepted Islam but who retained a human conscience — saw the Prophet ﷺ sitting in his garden, bleeding, alone. He sent his Christian slave, Addas, with a bunch of grapes.

Addas brought the grapes. The Prophet ﷺ took them, said *Bismillah*, and ate.

Addas had never heard those words said before eating. He looked at the man in front of him with sudden attention.

*"These are words that the people of this land do not say."*

*"And from what land are you, and what is your religion?"*

*"I am a Christian, from Nineveh."*

*"The city of the righteous man, Yunus ibn Matta?"*

Addas was astonished. *"How do you know Yunus ibn Matta?"*

*"He is my brother. He was a Prophet, and I am a Prophet."*

Addas knelt down. He kissed the Prophet's ﷺ hands and feet.

A Christian slave, in a garden outside a city that had just stoned the Prophet ﷺ until his feet bled, becoming the first person in Ta'if to recognize what the leaders of the city had refused to see. He accepted Islam on the spot.

The owners of the garden watched from a distance and commented to each other that Addas had been corrupted.

## The Angel of the Mountains

Then came a visitor of a different kind.

Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> appeared, and with him the Angel of the Mountains — a being assigned, as his name suggests, to the mountains.

The Angel of the Mountains greeted the Prophet ﷺ and said:

*"O Muhammad — Allah has heard what your people said to you. I am the Angel of the Mountains, and my Lord has sent me to you so that you may command me as you wish. If you wish, I will bring together the two mountains that stand above Ta'if and crush them between the two."*

The Prophet ﷺ was bleeding from his feet. He had been rejected by the city below. He had just made a dua of complete vulnerability. And now he had the power to end it — not metaphorically, but literally.

He said:

> *"No. Rather I hope that Allah will bring from their descendants people who will worship Allah alone and associate nothing with Him."*

The people who had stoned him. He refused punishment for them. He hoped for their children.

## What Ta'if Was

Years later, Aisha رضي الله عنها asked him: *"Was there ever a day harder for you than the day of Uhud?"*

He answered immediately: *"Your tribe gave me much trouble — and the worst was the day of al-Aqabah, when I presented myself to Ibn Abd Yalil ibn Abd Kulal and he did not respond as I had hoped. I went away, overwhelmed with grief, and did not recover until I reached Qarn al-Tha'alib."*

He described arriving there to find a cloud shading him, and Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> calling him — and what followed.

Ta'if was the worst day. Not because the physical pain was the greatest — Uhud would bring worse physical injury. But because of the completeness of the rejection at the moment of his greatest vulnerability. He had come after losing his wife, after losing his uncle, after losing his protection, at the moment when the mission most needed a new opening.

And the door had been slammed and stones thrown at his feet.

But what happened in the garden — the dua, the slave who kissed his hands, the angel he refused — shows us something about the Prophet ﷺ that no victory could have shown as clearly.

A man is most himself when he has nothing. And in that garden, with bleeding feet and nowhere left to go, he was entirely, completely himself.

Merciful. Trusting. Hoping for the children of the people who had just stoned him.

The Prophet ﷺ who would one day offer a general amnesty to the people of Mecca who had persecuted him for thirteen years was already fully present in the garden of Ta'if, refusing punishment for people who did not deserve his mercy.

He always had been.
