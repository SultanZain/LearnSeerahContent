## Nine Hundred and Fifty Years

If you were asked to do one thing — just one — for the rest of your life, knowing that almost no one would listen, knowing that the people you loved most might reject you, knowing that every year that passed would bring more mockery than acceptance — how long could you keep going?

A decade, perhaps. Maybe two.

Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> kept going for nine hundred and fifty years.

<Quran surah="29" ayah="14" />

His story is not just a story about a flood. It is a story about what unwavering commitment to Allah looks like when stretched across an almost incomprehensible span of time.

## How It Began

After Adam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and his children settled the earth, generations passed. Slowly, as they always do, people began to drift. Righteous men died, and those who loved them began to venerate their memories — first with statues, then with reverence, then with something that crossed the line into worship.

The names of those first idols are preserved in the Quran: Wadd, Suwa, Yaghuth, Ya'uq, and Nasr. Each one had once been a righteous man. Each one had become, across the slow passage of time, a god.

Into this world, Allah sent Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>.

His message was the same as every Prophet before and after him — the same as it would always be:

## Every Method, Every Day

What makes Nuh's <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> story remarkable is not just its length but its intensity. He did not settle into a comfortable routine of weekly reminders and seasonal sermons. He attacked the mission from every angle he could find.

He describes it himself in the Quran — in words that carry the exhaustion and the love of a man who has spent centuries pouring himself into a people:

<Quran surah="71" ayah="5-9" />

Night and day. Publicly and privately. Loud proclamation and quiet conversation. Argument and appeal. He varied every approach, tried every door, left no method untried.

And the response?

<Quran surah="71" ayah="7" />

They put their fingers in their ears. They pulled their garments over their faces. They walked away. And with each passing generation, the arrogance only deepened.

## The Dua That Ended an Era

After nine hundred and fifty years, Allah revealed to Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> that no more of his people would believe. The window had closed. The argument had been established beyond all doubt.

And Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — this man of extraordinary patience, who had never given up on his people across nearly a millennium — made a dua.

<Quran surah="71" ayah="26-27" />

This is a dua that can seem harsh to modern ears. But understand the context: Allah Himself had already told Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> that none of those remaining would ever believe. Every soul that would accept the truth already had. What remained on earth was only a generation that would produce more disbelief and more corruption, endlessly.

The dua was not cruelty. It was the closing of a chapter that Allah had already declared finished.

## Build the Ark

Then came a command unlike anything a Prophet had ever received.

*Build an ark.*

In the middle of the desert, far from any sea, an old man began constructing a massive vessel — by divine specification, plank by plank, under the guidance of Allah.

<Quran surah="11" ayah="37" />

The people who passed by mocked him openly. What was this old man building, and why? Their laughter echoed around the construction site for years. Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> told them simply: you will know soon enough, and when you do, it will be too late.

He kept building.

## The Flood

When the ark was complete, the sign came: water began to pour from an oven — a specific oven, designated by Allah as the signal that the time had arrived.

Allah commanded Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> to load the ark: a pair of every kind of creature, and the believers. The number of believers who boarded was small — profoundly, heartbreakingly small for a man who had given 950 years of his life to this people.

Then the sky opened. And the earth split open from below. Water came from above and below simultaneously, until everything was swallowed.

<Quran surah="54" ayah="11-12" />

## A Father's Grief

In the middle of the rising waters, Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> saw something that broke open a pain no length of patience could have prepared him for.

His son — his own son — was not on the ark.

Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> called out to him across the water: *My son, come aboard with us. Do not be with the disbelievers.*

His son called back: *I will take refuge on a mountain. It will protect me from the water.*

<Quran surah="11" ayah="43" />

There is no mountain high enough to escape what Allah has decreed.

Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, watching his son disappear beneath the waves, turned to Allah in grief and called out for him. And Allah, with mercy and firmness combined, told him: *He is not of your family. He is of those whose deeds were otherwise. Do not ask Me for what you have no knowledge of.*

It is one of the most devastating moments in all of Quranic narrative. A father who gave everything to his mission — and could not save his own child.

## The Reset

When the water receded and the ark came to rest on the mountain of Judi, the earth was silent. A world that had buried the truth under centuries of idol worship had been washed clean.

<Quran surah="11" ayah="44" />

Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and those with him descended onto a renewed earth — a second beginning for humanity, with Tawhid at its center once more.

He is called in the Quran *Nuh al-Awwal* — the first of the great Prophets of firm resolve. And the Prophet ﷺ told us that on the Day of Judgement, nations will come to Nuh <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and ask him to intercede, saying: *You are the first messenger Allah sent to the earth.*

His 950 years of patience, his grief, his unwavering call — none of it was wasted. It was all written. All witnessed. And all preserved for us to learn from, centuries later, in lessons exactly like this one.
