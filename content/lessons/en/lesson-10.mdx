## Kalimullah — The One Allah Spoke To

Among all the distinctions given to Prophets, one belonging to Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> stands in a class of its own.

Every Prophet received revelation — but through the angel Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, who carried the words of Allah to the Prophet's heart. Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was different. Allah spoke to him directly — without intermediary, without veil, in a direct address that no other Prophet before the final night journey of Muhammad ﷺ had experienced in the same way.

This is why he is called *Kalimullah* — the one Allah spoke to.

<Quran surah="4" ayah="164" />

His story begins, as so many great stories do, not with power — but with a basket on a river.

## The Child of Two Households

Pharaoh — the Firaun of Egypt — had been warned by his advisors and his own dreams that a child from the Children of Israel would rise to end his reign. His response was simple and brutal: kill every male child born to the Israelites.

Into this terror, Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was born.

His mother's love and her fear were both real. But Allah spoke to her heart with a command that must have seemed like madness:

<Quran surah="28" ayah="7" />

*Cast him into the river.* Trust Me.

She built a basket. She placed her infant inside. She set him on the Nile. And she let go.

The river carried him — as rivers carry what Allah directs — directly to the palace of Pharaoh. His wife Asiya <span className="font-ar">عليها السلام</span>, one of the greatest women in all of history, saw the basket pulled from the water and felt an immediate, overwhelming love for the child inside.

*Do not kill him,* she told Pharaoh. *Perhaps he will benefit us, or we will adopt him as a son.*

<Quran surah="28" ayah="9" />

The man who had ordered the slaughter of Israelite infants agreed to raise this Israelite child in his own home. The irony is so complete it could only be divine.

And Musa's <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> mother — through a chain of events orchestrated entirely by Allah — was brought in as his wet nurse, so that she nursed and raised her own son inside the palace of the very tyrant she had feared.

<Quran surah="28" ayah="13" />

## The Fugitive

Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> grew up in the palace — educated, privileged, carrying within him a deep awareness of who his people were and what they suffered.

One day he witnessed an Egyptian striking an Israelite. He intervened — struck the Egyptian — and the man died. It was not what he had intended. He turned immediately to Allah in remorse:

<Quran surah="28" ayah="16" />

But the incident became known. The order came for his arrest. And Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — raised in a palace, educated in the finest tradition of Egypt — fled into the desert as a fugitive with nothing.

He walked until he reached Madyan, exhausted and alone. There he helped two women water their flock at a well when the men around would not, then sat in the shade of a tree and made one of the most honest and simple duas in the Quran:

<Quran surah="28" ayah="24" />

*My Lord, I am in need of whatever good You might send down to me.*

No elaboration. No long petition. Just complete, transparent need placed before Allah. And Allah answered immediately — the women's father invited him in, gave him shelter, and eventually gave him his daughter in marriage. A fugitive with nothing became a settled man with a home, a wife, and years of quiet life ahead.

## The Burning Bush

Years passed in Madyan. Then came the night that changed everything.

Travelling with his family, Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> saw a fire on the side of a mountain. He left his family and approached — and as he drew near, a voice called out from the blessed valley of Tuwa:

<Quran surah="20" ayah="11-14" />

*I am Allah. There is no god but Me — so worship Me.*

The staff in his hand became a serpent. His hand emerged from his garment glowing white with light. And Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — who had spent his life moving from palace to exile to quiet obscurity — was given the heaviest commission any Prophet had yet received:

*Go to Pharaoh. He has transgressed all bounds.*

## Before the Throne of Pharaoh

Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> knew Pharaoh. He had grown up in his palace, eaten at his table, been raised under his roof. He knew better than anyone what kind of man sat on that throne.

He asked Allah for something before he went — the dua of a man who knows the weight of what lies ahead:

<Quran surah="20" ayah="25-28" />

*Expand my chest. Ease my task. Untie the knot in my tongue so they may understand my speech.*

Allah granted it all. And alongside him, Allah sent his brother Harun <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — more eloquent, to support what Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> carried.

They stood before the most powerful man on earth and delivered the simplest message: *Let the Children of Israel go. Worship Allah alone.*

Pharaoh scoffed. He called Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> a madman, a sorcerer, a man biting the hand that had fed him. He assembled his greatest magicians to publicly defeat this challenger.

The magicians threw their ropes and staffs — and through their craft, made them appear as serpents, filling the ground with the illusion of writhing creatures.

Then Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> cast his staff.

It became a real serpent — and consumed everything the magicians had produced. The illusion swallowed by the reality.

The magicians — men who understood exactly what they had seen and what it meant — fell to their knees immediately.

<Quran surah="26" ayah="46-48" />

*We believe in the Lord of the worlds — the Lord of Musa and Harun.*

## The Plagues and the Exodus

Pharaoh did not relent. Pride and power have their own gravity, and they pull men deeper even when they know they are sinking.

Allah sent sign after sign upon Egypt — floods, locusts, lice, frogs, blood. Each time, Pharaoh promised to release the Israelites if the affliction was lifted. Each time the affliction passed, he broke his promise.

Then came the final night. Allah commanded Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> to lead his people out of Egypt under cover of darkness. Centuries of slavery — ended in a single night, by divine decree.

Pharaoh, when he realized what had happened, pursued them with his army. The Israelites reached the sea. Behind them, the thunder of hooves. Before them, water in every direction.

<Quran surah="26" ayah="61-63" />

*Strike the sea with your staff.*

The sea split. Twelve paths opened through the water, one for each tribe. The Israelites crossed on dry ground.

Pharaoh followed. And when his army was fully within the parted sea, the waters closed.

<Quran surah="10" ayah="92" />

Allah preserved Pharaoh's body — so that it would be a sign for those who came after. And to this day, the mummified remains of ancient Egyptian Pharaohs lie in museums, exactly as Allah promised.

## At the Mountain

Free at last, the Children of Israel traveled to the Sinai. And Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> ascended the mountain to receive the Torah — forty nights alone with Allah, receiving the most comprehensive law the world had yet seen.

It was on this mountain that Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> made the most audacious request any creation has ever made of the Creator:

<Quran surah="7" ayah="143" />

*My Lord — show me, that I may look at You.*

The request came not from arrogance but from a love so overwhelming it had become a longing beyond words. And Allah answered with a lesson:

*You will not see Me. But look at the mountain — if it remains in its place, then you will see Me.*

Allah's manifestation touched the mountain. It crumbled to dust. Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> fell unconscious. When he recovered, trembling, he said: *Glory be to You. I repent to You. I am the first of the believers.*

## The Weight of His Legacy

Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> descended from the mountain carrying the tablets of the Torah. He returned to find his people had already — in the weeks of his absence — begun worshipping a golden calf constructed by a man named Samiri.

The grief and fury of that moment, the patience required to rebuild, the decades of leading a people through the wilderness who complained, doubted, and demanded — all of it is preserved in the Quran in detail.

He carried them. He argued with them. He interceded for them. He never abandoned them.

The Prophet ﷺ said:

> *"May Allah have mercy on Musa — he was harmed with more than this, and he was patient."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, 3405

On the night of the Isra and Mi'raj — the Prophet's ﷺ night journey — when Muhammad ﷺ ascended through the heavens, it was Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> he encountered weeping in the sixth heaven, grieving that more of the Prophet's ﷺ ummah would enter Jannah than his own. And it was Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> who kept sending the Prophet ﷺ back to ask for a reduction in the daily prayers — out of concern for the ummah he had never met.

That is who Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was. Not just a lawgiver. Not just a liberator. A man of deep, aching, unstoppable care for the people Allah placed in his trust.
