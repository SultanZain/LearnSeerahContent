## The Machine of Opposition

Quraysh did not panic immediately after the announcement on Safa. They were experienced political and social operators — they had managed the complex tribal dynamics of Arabia for generations. They assessed the threat and responded with the full toolkit of a society that knew how to protect its interests.

What they were protecting was substantial. The Kaaba was the source of Mecca's prestige and prosperity. The pilgrimage season brought trade from across Arabia. The social hierarchy that placed the nobility of Quraysh at its peak depended on the existing religious order. And the tribal pride that defined every free man's identity was directly challenged by a message that said all human beings stood equal before one God.

The opposition was not irrational. It was, from their perspective, entirely logical.

## What They Did to the Prophet ﷺ

Against the Prophet ﷺ himself, Quraysh had a problem: he was protected by Abu Talib and by the code of tribal honor that prevented one clan from killing a member of another without triggering a blood feud.

So they used other methods.

They mocked him in the marketplace. Abu Jahl — one of the sharpest minds and most bitter opponents among the Quraysh leadership — would follow him through the streets calling him a liar in public. They composed poetry ridiculing him. They spread slander — calling him a *kahin* (soothsayer), a *sha'ir* (poet), a *majnun* (one possessed by jinn). Each label was designed to pre-explain what people were hearing when they heard the Quran: this is not revelation, this is madness or trickery.

They sent delegations to Abu Talib, repeatedly, demanding that he withdraw his protection. They offered to replace Muhammad ﷺ with any young man of Quraysh Abu Talib chose — they would treat him as a son. Abu Talib refused every time, though he himself never accepted Islam.

They offered the Prophet ﷺ himself everything — wealth, leadership, the most beautiful women in Arabia — in exchange for silence. He refused with the words that have echoed through history: *"If they placed the sun in my right hand and the moon in my left, I would not abandon this matter."*

## What They Did to the Believers

Against those without tribal protection, there was no restraint.

The slaves who had accepted Islam were the most exposed. Their masters — powerful men of Quraysh who felt both religiously offended and economically threatened — made examples of them.

Yasir ibn Amir <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> and his wife Sumayyah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span> were among the first to endure what Mecca was capable of. They were not from powerful families. They had no protection. They were taken to the burning sand of the desert in the midday heat and tortured — day after day — to make them recant.

The Prophet ﷺ would pass by them as they were being tortured and could do nothing but say:

*"Patience, O family of Yasir. Your appointment is Jannah."*

Sumayyah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span> refused to recant. Abu Jahl killed her with a spear — making her the first martyr in the history of Islam.

Yasir <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> died under the torture. Their son Ammar <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> endured what his parents had endured, and — in a moment of unbearable suffering — spoke the words of disbelief with his tongue while his heart remained full of faith. He came to the Prophet ﷺ weeping and told him what he had said.

The verse came: *Except for one who is forced while his heart is at rest with faith.* Ammar رضي الله عنه had not disbelieved. He had survived. And the Quran had already answered the question of his conscience.

## The Slander Campaign

For the pilgrimage seasons — when thousands of Arabs from across the peninsula came to Mecca — Quraysh organized something that would today be recognized as a coordinated information campaign.

Al-Walid ibn al-Mughira — the greatest literary mind in Mecca, a man who had privately acknowledged the Quran's superiority — was tasked with creating a counter-narrative. What should they call Muhammad ﷺ to warn the pilgrims?

He rejected each suggestion until they settled on *sahir* — sorcerer. Not because it was accurate, but because it was the label most likely to create a barrier between the pilgrims and the Quran's direct impact.

Delegations were organized at the entry points to Mecca during Hajj season. Every road that pilgrims traveled was staffed with men of Quraysh, warning the newcomers: there is a man here who has broken up families, who practices sorcery with his words — do not listen to him.

The strategy worked on many. Some turned away before they heard. Others heard but with ears already stopped by the warning.

But some heard anyway. And what they heard, they could not unhear. The pilgrimage season — designed by Quraysh as a tool of suppression — became, year by year, one of the primary channels through which the message of Islam reached the rest of Arabia.

## The Ones Who Held

Through all of it — the mockery, the torture, the social exclusion, the economic pressure, the slander — the early believers held.

Not because they were impervious to pain. Not because the cost was small. But because something had happened to them that no amount of Quraysh pressure could undo: they had heard the truth, recognized it, and accepted it. And once a soul has genuinely accepted truth, the options available to its opponents narrow dramatically.

You can torture the body. You cannot torture the certainty out of a soul that has genuinely found what it was looking for.

This is what Quraysh could not understand, and what would ultimately defeat them. They were operating on the assumption that the believers' conviction was a social or economic calculation that could be reversed by making the calculation unfavorable. They were wrong. The conviction was not a calculation at all.
