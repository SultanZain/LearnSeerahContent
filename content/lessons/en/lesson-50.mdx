## The Empty Lot Between the Date Palms

The site the camel had chosen was not much to look at.

An open area — used partly as a drying place for dates, partly as a burial ground for the pre-Islamic dead of the neighborhood. Date palms growing at its edges. The ground uneven and in places difficult.

The Prophet ﷺ looked at it and saw a mosque.

But first — the land had to be purchased.

{/* page */}

## Buying What Could Have Been Given

The lot belonged to two orphan brothers — Sahl and Suhayl, sons of Amr — who were in the care of As'ad ibn Zurara <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>. When the Prophet ﷺ made known his intention to build his mosque there, the boys came forward immediately.

*"It is yours, O Messenger of Allah. Take it."*

He refused.

He would not build his mosque on land taken without fair compensation — not from orphans, not from anyone. The price was agreed. It was paid. And only then did the work begin.

The pre-Islamic graves that occupied part of the site were moved. The date palms were cleared — their trunks set aside to become pillars for the mosque's structure. The ground was leveled.

And the Prophet ﷺ picked up a brick.

## Building Together

The construction of the Prophet's Mosque was a communal act in the fullest sense of the word.

Every hand available was at work. The muhajirin who had arrived from Mecca. The Ansar of Medina. The Prophet ﷺ himself, carrying mud bricks and stones alongside them, refusing to be separated from the labor of building what would be his mosque and his home.

As they worked, they chanted — the work chant that the sources have preserved:

*"O Allah, there is no good except the good of the next life — so have mercy on the Ansar and the muhajirin."*

The Prophet ﷺ chanted with them. He carried bricks. When companions tried to take his load, he refused. A man came to him and said: *"O Messenger of Allah — let me carry that for you."* He said: *"Go and take another. You are not more in need of reward before Allah than I am."*

He worked until the mosque was complete.

## The Mosque That Was Built

When it was done, the mosque was simple.

Its walls were of mud brick. Its roof was of palm leaves and branches — a material that let rain through, and which was later improved as the community's resources grew. Its floor was bare earth. Its pillars were the trunks of date palms, set into the ground.

On the east side of the mosque, the Prophet ﷺ built a row of small rooms — *hujurat* — one for each of his wives. Simple, small, attached to the mosque itself. The door of Aisha's <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span> room would open directly into the mosque. The line between his private life and his public mission was, architecturally, almost nonexistent.

On the north side, a shaded area — the Suffah — was set aside. This would become the home of the Ahl al-Suffah, the poor companions رضي الله عنهم who had nowhere else to go.

The qiblah — the direction of prayer — was initially set toward Jerusalem, as it had been since the beginning of Islam. It would change seventeen months after the Hijrah, when the revelation came to turn toward Mecca. The mosque's architecture accommodated this change.

## The Center of Everything

From the day it was completed, the Prophet's Mosque was the center of the new community in every dimension.

The five daily prayers were led there by the Prophet ﷺ himself — a practice he maintained throughout his years in Medina, missing the congregational prayer only when illness prevented him. The Jumu'ah was established there every Friday. The two Eids were celebrated there.

But it was also the seat of governance. Delegations from tribes and nations came to the mosque to meet the Prophet ﷺ. Treaties were negotiated there. Disputes among the Muslims were brought there for judgment. The administrative decisions of the growing Muslim state were made in that simple building with its palm-leaf roof.

It was also the school. The Prophet ﷺ taught there — in formal sessions and informal ones. Companions رضي الله عنهم sat in his presence and absorbed every word. The Ahl al-Suffah were there constantly, learning by proximity what those with homes and families could only learn in intervals.

And it was a shelter. When someone had nowhere to go, the mosque received them. When the wounded needed care, they were tended there. When a stranger came to Medina with no connection and no host, the mosque was where they could be until the community organized their needs.

## The Ahl al-Suffah

Among the most remarkable features of the Prophet's Mosque was the people who lived in it.

The Ahl al-Suffah — the People of the Bench — were companions رضي الله عنهم, many of them muhajirin, who had come from Mecca with nothing and had no means of establishing a home in Medina. They slept in the covered area of the mosque. They had no steady income. They depended on the charity of the community — on what the Prophet ﷺ sent them, on what those with means contributed.

Among them were men who would become the greatest transmitters of hadith in Islamic history. Abu Hurairah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> — who narrated more hadith than any other companion — spent a significant period with the Ahl al-Suffah, his constant proximity to the Prophet ﷺ giving him a wealth of transmitted knowledge that his situation, paradoxically, enabled.

The Prophet ﷺ never let them go hungry if he had anything to give. When food came — a gift, a spoil of war, a charitable donation — he directed portions to the Ahl al-Suffah. When the community had little, they shared the little.

The mosque of the Prophet ﷺ was not a building that kept the poor at a respectful distance. It was a building that made the poor its residents — and in doing so, made the entire community responsible for their welfare in the most immediate and undeniable way possible.

## What Remains

The mosque that the Prophet ﷺ built on that empty lot in Medina was approximately thirty by thirty-five meters. Its roof let in rain. Its floor was bare earth.

Today, the Prophet's Mosque is one of the largest in the world — capable of holding millions of worshippers at peak capacity, its green dome visible from across the city, its minarets rising above the Medina skyline.

But somewhere beneath the marble and the air conditioning and the vast carpeted halls is the earth where the Prophet ﷺ stood and led the companions رضي الله عنهم in prayer. The spot where Abu Hurairah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> slept, hungry, with his face pressed to the ground to suppress the pain of it. The spot where delegations from Arabia and Persia and Abyssinia came and were received by a man sitting in a simple mosque under a roof of palm leaves.

And in its southeast corner — the area called al-Rawdah al-Sharifah, the Noble Garden — is the most beloved spot on earth after the Kaaba itself: the space between the Prophet's ﷺ pulpit and his grave, about which he said:

> *"What is between my house and my pulpit is one of the gardens of Paradise."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, 1196

The mosque that was built in a few weeks by travelers and refugees is the second holiest place in Islam. The measure of a building is not its architecture — it is the intention of the hands that built it and the quality of what was done within it.

Those hands built on taqwa. And what was done within it changed the world.
