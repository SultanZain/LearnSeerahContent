## A Common Misunderstanding

When people encounter Islam for the first time — or when Muslims themselves think about their faith without much reflection — there is an assumption quietly sitting in the background: that Islam began with Muhammad ﷺ, in Arabia, in the 7th century.

That Islam is, in other words, a historical religion with a historical starting point.

This assumption is understandable. But it is wrong.

Islam — true submission to Allah alone — is not a religion that was invented. It is a religion that was *restored*.

## Before the Beginning

Before any human being set foot on this earth, before the first breath was drawn or the first word spoken, Allah gathered every soul that would ever exist and addressed them directly.

<Quran surah="7" ayah="172" />

Every soul — yours, mine, every person who has ever lived — answered: *Yes. We testify.*

This is called the *Mithaq* — the primordial covenant. It is why, when a person hears the truth stated clearly, something in them often recognizes it before they can explain why. The soul has heard this before. It answered this question before it was born.

Islam, then, does not ask you to accept something foreign. It asks you to remember something ancient.

## The Fitra: What Every Soul Already Knows

This primordial covenant left a mark. Allah built into every human being a natural disposition — an inclination toward recognizing that a Creator exists, that He deserves worship, that truth and justice are real.

This disposition is called *Fitra*.

The Prophet ﷺ described it:

> *"Every child is born upon the Fitra. Then his parents make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Magian."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, 1358

The default state of the human soul is not disbelief. It is not a blank slate. It is an innate orientation toward Allah — one that the environment, the upbringing, and the accumulated pressures of life can bury, but never entirely erase.

This is why, even among people who have never heard of Islam, you find the same instincts surfacing: the sense that something greater exists, the recognition that gratitude toward a Creator is natural, the feeling that pure atheism leaves something unanswered at the deepest level of experience.

That is the Fitra speaking.

## Adam عليه السلام: The First Muslim

The first human being was the first Muslim.

Adam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> did not practice Judaism, Christianity, or any other historically named religion. He knew Allah directly. He submitted to Him. He taught his children to do the same.

Islam, in its essence, is simply this: *submission to Allah alone.* And that submission began the moment the first human soul drew breath.

Every Prophet who came after Adam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> was not founding something new. He was returning to something original — clearing away the layers of distortion that had accumulated, and restoring the pure center that humanity keeps drifting away from.

<Quran surah="3" ayah="19" />

{/* page */}

## Ibrahim عليه السلام: The Model of Return

Of all the Prophets, the Quran singles out one as the greatest embodiment of this primordial Islam: Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>.

He was born into a civilization saturated with idol worship. His own father carved and sold the idols. And yet, through the pure exercise of his God-given reason and his Fitra, Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> looked at the stars, the moon, and the sun — and rejected each one as they set and disappeared. He arrived, on his own, at the conclusion that only the One who made all of these deserved worship.

<Quran surah="6" ayah="79" />

The Quran calls him *Hanif* — one who has turned away from all falsehood and inclined purely toward the truth. Not a Jew. Not a Christian. Not a member of any later historical tradition.

<Quran surah="3" ayah="67" />

Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> is held up in the Quran as the model precisely because he represents what every soul is capable of: returning to the original, cutting through the noise of inherited falsehood, and finding Allah.

## What Muhammad ﷺ Actually Came to Do

This reframes everything about how we understand the mission of Muhammad ﷺ.

He did not arrive with a new idea. He did not invent a religion to compete with the ones before it. He came as the final messenger of the oldest religion in existence — the same submission to Allah that Adam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> lived, that Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> revived, that Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> carried to their peoples.

What was different about his coming was not the message. It was the finality and the preservation.

Every previous scripture had been revealed for a specific people in a specific time — and over time, human hands altered them. The Torah was changed. The Injeel was lost in its original form. But the Quran was revealed to be preserved — word for word, letter for letter — until the Last Day.

And Muhammad ﷺ was sent not to one tribe or one nation, but to all of humanity, for all of time.

<Quran surah="34" ayah="28" />

## The Circle Closes

And so the picture becomes clear.

The religion of Allah is one. It has always been one. From the first human being to walk this earth to the last one who will, the invitation has never changed: *turn to your Lord, submit to Him alone, and live accordingly.*

Every Prophet was a chapter in this single story. Every scripture was a verse in this single book. And the Quran — the final revelation, preserved and complete — is the closing of that book, with nothing left to add and nothing needing correction.

When we study the Seerah of Muhammad ﷺ, we are not studying the origin of an alien religion. We are studying the culmination of a story that began before history itself — a story that every human soul, at its deepest level, already knows.

We are, in a very real sense, coming home.
