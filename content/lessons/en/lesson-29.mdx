## An Injustice in the Marketplace

The immediate cause of one of the most remarkable events in pre-Islamic Mecca was a simple transaction that went wrong.

A merchant from Yemen — a man called al-Zubaydi in some narrations — arrived in Mecca with goods to sell. He found a buyer: al-As ibn Wa'il al-Sahmi, one of the powerful figures of Quraysh. Al-As purchased the goods, took them, and refused to pay.

The Yemeni had no tribal connections in Mecca. Without a tribe, he had no protection and no leverage. He went to the leaders of Quraysh one by one, explaining what had happened, asking for help.

No one moved. Al-As was powerful. Getting involved meant making an enemy.

The Yemeni merchant climbed the hill of Abu Qubays — visible from across Mecca — and called out in the tradition of those who had been wronged and received no justice. He improvised verses of poetry, describing what had been done to him, and asked the people of this sacred city to help.

His voice carried across Mecca. It reached ears that were not entirely deaf.

## The Gathering at Ibn Judan's House

Several clans of Quraysh — among them Banu Hashim, Banu Muttalib, Banu Asad, Banu Zuhrah, and Banu Taym — were moved by what they heard. Whether from genuine moral feeling, or from the shame of being seen to tolerate injustice in the city that hosted the Kaaba, or from both — they acted.

They gathered at the house of Abdullah ibn Judan, one of the respected elders of Mecca, and they made a pledge.

*We will be one hand with the oppressed against the oppressor. We will restore to every person what is rightfully theirs — whether he is from among us or from outside of us — as long as one of us remains alive.*

The alliance took its name — *Hilf al-Fudul*, the Pact of the Virtuous — from three men named Fadl who had made a similar pact in Arabia's distant past. It was a deliberate evocation of a tradition of justice older than the Jahiliyyah's most recent corruptions.

After the pact was formed, they went as a group to al-As ibn Wa'il and demanded he return what he owed the Yemeni. Al-As, faced with a coalition he could not politically withstand, paid.

The Yemeni returned to Yemen with his money. And Mecca had done something unusual — something the Jahiliyyah rarely managed: it had chosen justice over power.

## He Was There

Muhammad ﷺ was there.

He was young — approximately fifteen to twenty years old, depending on the narration. Not yet a man of standing in Quraysh. Not yet Al-Amin in the fullest sense that the title would later carry.

But he was at the gathering in the house of Abdullah ibn Judan. He heard the pledge. He joined his voice to it.

And years later — after revelation had come, after prophethood, after the transformation of Arabia — he spoke about that night with a warmth that time had not diminished:

> *"I witnessed in the house of Abdullah ibn Judan a pact that I would not exchange for red camels. And if I were called to it in Islam, I would respond."*
> — Sirah Ibn Hisham

Red camels — the most valuable possession in pre-Islamic Arabia, the ultimate measure of wealth. He would not trade that pact for all of them.

And if someone, in the era of Islam, invoked that pact — calling on him to stand with the oppressed — he would come.

## What This Tells Us

The Prophet's ﷺ statement about Hilf al-Fudul is one of the most quietly significant things he ever said — and it is often passed over quickly in the Seerah because it does not involve armies or miracles or dramatic confrontations.

But sit with it.

He is saying that a pre-Islamic pledge — made before revelation, by men who were not Muslims, in the name of tribal honor rather than divine command — carries a moral weight that Islam confirms rather than cancels.

Not because the men who made it were guided by revelation. But because the principle at its heart — the protection of the oppressed, the refusal to allow power to devour the powerless — is a principle so fundamental to the nature of justice that it belongs to the fitrah, the primordial human orientation toward right.

Islam did not come to erase every value that existed before it. It came to purify, to complete, to redirect. The love of truth, the protection of the weak, the honoring of one's word — these were not invented by Islam. They were confirmed by it, grounded in their proper source, and freed from the distortions that tribal pride and the Jahiliyyah had introduced.

<Quran surah="16" ayah="90" />

*Indeed, Allah commands justice, kindness, and giving to relatives, and He forbids immorality, wrongdoing, and oppression.*

Hilf al-Fudul was imperfect and tribal. But in its core impulse — standing with the oppressed — it was pointing in the same direction that Allah had always been pointing.

## The Man This Reveals

The young man who joined Hilf al-Fudul was not attending a political event. He was not calculating its social benefits or its tribal implications.

He joined because a man had been cheated, had cried out for help, and no one who could help had acted. That was enough for him.

This is the pattern that runs through every stage of his life before the mission — and every stage of the mission itself.

The orphan without recourse. The widow without support. The slave without rights. The stranger without a tribe. The poor without advocates. Every time one of these appeared in his field of vision, something in him responded — not as policy, not as religious obligation, but as instinct.

He was, before he was anything else, a man who could not stand to see the powerless suffer without trying to do something about it.

The revelation would give this instinct its full theological grounding. The mission would give it its full social and political expression. The Quran would confirm, in verse after verse, that this instinct was not accidental but divine.

But the instinct was already there. It had been there from before the first verse was revealed. It had been there on the night in the house of Abdullah ibn Judan, when a young man from Banu Hashim raised his hand and pledged to stand with the oppressed for as long as he lived.

He kept that pledge for the rest of his life. And the revelation that came to him simply told him — in the most complete and luminous possible terms — why it had always been right to do so.
