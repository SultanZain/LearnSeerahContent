## A Mountain Above the City

Jabal al-Nur — the Mountain of Light — rises above Mecca to the north, visible from the city below. It is not the tallest mountain around Mecca, nor the most impressive. But near its summit there is a cave so small that a man sitting inside it can barely stretch his legs, with an opening so narrow that the sky above Mecca is only a sliver from within.

Muhammad ﷺ had been going there for years.

Not occasionally. Not casually. With the regularity and the intentionality of a man who had found, in that small dark space above the city, something he could not find anywhere else.

What was he looking for?

## What the City Could Not Give

By his late thirties, Muhammad ﷺ had everything that Mecca measured success by. A noble lineage. A respected wife. A reputation — *Al-Amin* — that opened every door. A household that functioned well and children who were healthy.

And none of it was enough.

Not because he was ungrateful. Not because he was depressed in the modern clinical sense. But because something in him — the same Fitra that had looked at the idols of his father's city and felt their wrongness from childhood, the same quality that had kept him untouched by the moral failures of the Jahiliyyah for forty years — was looking for something that the world around him simply did not contain.

The Kaaba was full of idols. The city was full of noise. The markets were full of transactions. And the sky above Hira was full of silence.

He would pack provisions — dates, a little water — enough for days. He would climb the mountain alone. He would sit in the cave. And he would contemplate.

The Arabic word the sources use is *tahannuth* — a word that carries the sense of deep, sustained spiritual seeking, the worship of Allah in the way that fitrah alone could guide, before revelation had given it its precise form.

## The True Dreams

In the six months before the first revelation, something began.

Aisha رضي الله عنها — the most meticulous narrator of the Prophet's ﷺ private life — preserved this detail with precision: the beginning of revelation for the Prophet ﷺ was through true dreams.

Every dream he saw came true. Not approximately, not symbolically, but exactly — *like the breaking of dawn*, the narration says, with the clarity and completeness of a sky that shifts from dark to light with a precision no human clock can match.

Six months of this. Six months of a man who went to sleep and woke with the knowledge that what he had seen in his sleep was real — would be real, had already been written.

The scholars of the Seerah have reflected on this period with care. Why dreams first? Why not begin with the full weight of the revelation?

Because a soul being prepared for the descent of the Quran needed preparation. The door of the unseen does not blow open all at once without consequences. Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> asked to see Allah and fell unconscious when a mountain could not bear the manifestation. The revelation that was coming to Muhammad ﷺ would require a soul that had been gently, gradually introduced to the reality of the divine before the full encounter.

The true dreams were that introduction. A gentle opening of the aperture — a little more light, a little more light — until the night when the light arrived in full.

## Ramadan on the Mountain

The month of Ramadan was his longest retreat. He would take enough provisions for the whole month, climb Hira, and remain.

Khadijah رضي الله عنها would send food up to him. Sometimes she would climb herself to check on him. The mountain became, in those years, the real geography of his inner life — more real, in some ways, than the city below.

He was forty years old when the month of Ramadan arrived in the year that everything changed.

He climbed as he always had. He settled into the cave as he always had. He sat with the silence and the sky and the search that had occupied him for years.

And then — in the last ten nights of Ramadan, when the darkness of the cave and the darkness of the night outside had merged into a single quality of deep stillness — something came.

Not as he had imagined. Not as anyone could have imagined.

The cave of Hira was about to stop being a place where a man searched for the divine. It was about to become the place where the divine found the man.

In our next lesson, we enter that moment — the most important night in human history since the night of Adam's <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> creation. The night of *Iqra*.
