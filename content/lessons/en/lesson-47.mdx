## The Night They Left

Mecca was asleep — or appeared to be.

The Prophet ﷺ had spent his last night in the city of his birth doing something that speaks to the entirety of his character: he asked Ali <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> to return, in the days after his departure, every trust that had been deposited with him. Every valuable. Every item that the people of Mecca — including his enemies — had left with Al-Amin for safekeeping.

The man leaving his city under threat of assassination was concerned with his obligations to the people trying to kill him.

He went to Abu Bakr's <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> house through the back. Abu Bakr wept — the tears of a man who had waited for this moment and now that it had come felt the full weight of what was beginning.

They left in the dark. Not north, toward Medina. South, toward the mountains.

## Three Days in Thawr

The cave of Thawr sat near the top of a mountain south of Mecca — a climb of approximately an hour through difficult terrain, in the dark, with Quraysh's armed men having left the Prophet's ﷺ house empty-handed and furious.

They climbed. They entered the cave.

Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> went in first — checking for snakes, for dangers, covering any holes he found with pieces of his garment. A narration says he was bitten by something he had covered with his foot, and did not move or make a sound so as not to disturb the Prophet ﷺ who was resting against him.

Three days. The system Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> had prepared ran quietly around them: Abdullah his son came each night with news of Quraysh's movements and plans. Asma <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span> his daughter came each day with food — the provisions tied in two strips torn from her belt. Amir ibn Fuhayra, a freed slave, drove sheep past the cave each evening to cover their footprints with the tracks of the flock.

On the second day, the trackers reached the entrance of the cave.

Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> looked down at their feet — so close. He whispered: *if one of them looks down, he will see us.*

*Do not grieve — indeed Allah is with us.*

The trackers saw the spider's web. The dove's nest. They turned back.

## The Pursuer Who Became a Witness

On the coastal road heading north, with Abdullah ibn Urayqit guiding them through terrain that avoided the main routes — a rider appeared behind them.

Suraqah ibn Malik of Banu Mudlij had heard about the hundred-camel reward. He had made his calculation and set out in pursuit. He was skilled at tracking and he was gaining ground.

He drew close.

His horse's legs sank into the sand.

He freed them. He urged the horse forward. The legs sank again.

A third time.

Suraqah understood the sign better than any argument could have made him understand. He called out:

*"O Muhammad — call upon your Lord to free my horse, and I give you my covenant that I will not harm you and that I will turn back anyone who comes after you."*

The Prophet ﷺ made dua. The horse's legs came free.

Suraqah turned to go. Then the Prophet ﷺ said something to him that he would carry for the rest of his life — through the Hijrah, through the battles, through the conquest of Mecca, through the Ridda wars, all the way to the caliphate of Umar <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>:

*"How will you feel, O Suraqah, when you wear the bracelets of Khusraw?"*

The bracelets of the Persian emperor. A Bedouin tribesman of the Hijaz, hearing a prophecy about himself from a fugitive riding a camel with a hundred-camel price on his head.

He went back. He turned away those who came after. And years later, when the Persian Empire had fallen and the bracelets of Khusraw were brought to Umar <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> as spoils, Umar called Suraqah forward and placed them on his wrists — in the literal fulfillment of what a fugitive had promised on a desert road.

## Quba — The First Stop

After approximately two weeks of travel — the journey was slow, careful, and deliberate — the Prophet ﷺ arrived at the outskirts of Medina. He stopped first at Quba, a settlement on the southern edge of the city, where Banu Amr ibn Awf lived.

He stayed at Quba for several days — some narrations say three, others say more. And during those days, he did something that would have permanent significance in Islamic history.

He built a mosque.

The first mosque ever built in Islam — constructed with the Prophet's ﷺ own hands, alongside his companions رضي الله عنهم, in the settlement of Quba before he had even entered the main city. The Quran would later describe it as the mosque founded from the first day on taqwa — on God-consciousness.

<Quran surah="9" ayah="108" />

The Prophet ﷺ said about Quba:

> *"Whoever purifies himself in his house and then comes to the Masjid of Quba and prays in it — he will have the reward of Umrah."*
> — Sunan Ibn Majah, 1412

## The Arrival

When he finally entered Medina proper — riding his she-camel Qaswa, approaching through the streets of a city that had been waiting — what happened was not a formal ceremony or a political reception.

It was something more human than that.

The entire city came out.

Men lined the roads. Women climbed to the rooftops. Children ran alongside the camel. The women and children of the Ansar are said to have sung:

*Tala'a al-Badru 'alayna — the full moon has risen over us, from the valley of Wada'.*

A city greeting its Prophet not with protocol but with joy — the unceremonious, genuine joy of people who had been waiting for something and had finally seen it arrive.

Every family of the Ansar wanted him to stay with them. He passed through them, nodding, loving them for it — and telling them to let the camel walk. She would stop where she was meant to stop. The choice of where he would stay was not his to make.

## What the Hijrah Was

The migration from Mecca to Medina — approximately 450 kilometers, traveled over weeks on the back of a camel — is the event that anchors the Islamic calendar itself. The Muslim year begins not from the birth of the Prophet ﷺ, not from the first revelation, but from the Hijrah.

This choice is not incidental. It reflects something the early Muslim community understood about the nature of this event.

The revelation had come in Mecca. But Islam — as a lived community, as a functioning social and political reality, as the complete expression of divine guidance in human life — began in Medina. The Hijrah was not a retreat. It was a transition. From the private to the public. From the persecuted minority to the established community. From thirteen years of bearing to the beginning of building.

The Prophet ﷺ had told his companions رضي الله عنهم:

> *"I was shown the land to which you will migrate — I saw a land with date palms between two lava fields."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, 3926

He had seen it before he went. He had known where he was going. And now, after the cave and the desert and the pursuer and the coastal road and Quba — he was there.

The city was ready. The work was beginning. And a man who had spent thirteen years in his own city with his feet bled on its stones and his name called a liar in its streets — was being received by another city as if he were the full moon rising over the valley.

Which, in every meaningful sense, he was.
