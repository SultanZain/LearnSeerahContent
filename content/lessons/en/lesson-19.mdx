## The Boy Who Became a Legend

When al-Muttalib rode into Mecca with his nephew Shaybah — the boy the people mistook for his slave — no one could have predicted what this child would become.

Abdul-Muttalib ibn Hashim grew into perhaps the most commanding figure Mecca had seen since his grandfather Qusayy. He became the chief of Quraysh, the guardian of the Kaaba, and the man whose name would be attached forever to the grandfather of the Prophet ﷺ. But his story begins not with authority — it begins with a dream and a shovel.

## The Rediscovery of Zamzam

The well of Zamzam — which had burst from beneath the feet of Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> as an infant in the barren valley of Mecca — had, over the centuries, been buried and forgotten. Its location was lost. Mecca drew its water from other sources.

Then Abdul-Muttalib began to dream.

Night after night, a voice in his sleep commanded him: *Dig Taybah.* Then: *Dig Barrah.* Then: *Dig Zamzam.* Each time, the dream grew more specific, pointing him to a particular spot in the Masjid al-Haram, between two idols called Isaf and Na'ilah.

He obeyed. He went to the spot with his son al-Harith — his only son at the time — and began to dig. The people of Quraysh gathered to watch and to mock. What was this man doing, digging between the idols of the Haram?

Abdul-Muttalib kept digging.

Then the earth gave way. And Zamzam — ancient, cold, rushing — burst forth from beneath the ground, exactly where the dream had said.

The well of Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, silent for centuries, was alive again. And Abdul-Muttalib, the man who had persisted through mockery on the instruction of a dream, was now the lord of the most sacred water in Arabia.

## The Vow That Nearly Cost Everything

In that moment of profound gratitude — standing over the rediscovered well of the Prophets — Abdul-Muttalib made a vow.

He had only one son. He had always longed for more. And in his overwhelmed state, he promised Allah: *if You give me ten sons who live to adulthood, I will sacrifice one of them at the Kaaba.*

Allah gave him ten sons. And when the last of them reached manhood, Abdul-Muttalib remembered his vow.

He gathered his sons and explained what he had promised. Not one of them refused or flinched — they were their father's sons. He drew lots. The lot fell on Abdullah. His youngest. His most beloved. The one who, of all his children, bore the greatest resemblance to a soul untouched by the coarseness of his age.

Abdul-Muttalib took Abdullah by the hand and walked toward the Kaaba with a knife.

The people of Mecca rose as one. *You cannot do this.* His daughters wept. The men of Quraysh blocked his path. Even those who had no great love for Abdul-Muttalib could not stand by and watch.

They persuaded him to seek counsel. He traveled to a wise woman in Yathrib who told him: *draw lots between your son and ten camels. If the lot falls on the camels, slaughter them. If it falls on your son, add ten more camels and draw again — keep adding until the camels are chosen.*

The lots were drawn. Again and again, the lot fell on Abdullah. They kept adding camels — ten at a time — until they reached one hundred. At one hundred camels, the lot finally fell on the camels.

Abdul-Muttalib slaughtered all one hundred. Abdullah lived.

And Abdullah ibn Abdul-Muttalib — the young man the lot had tried to take ten times — would become the father of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

## The Guardian Who Stood Alone

The rediscovery of Zamzam brought Abdul-Muttalib prestige. The near-sacrifice of Abdullah showed his people the depth of his commitment to his word. But the event that revealed his soul most completely came when an army appeared on the horizon.

We will tell that story fully in the next lesson. But what must be understood now is how Abdul-Muttalib faced it.

When Abraha's army — tens of thousands of soldiers, led by war elephants — marched toward Mecca to destroy the Kaaba, Abdul-Muttalib led his people into the mountains. Not to fight. Not to flee in fear. But to leave the House of Allah to its Owner.

He himself went to Abraha's camp to negotiate the return of his camels that Abraha's men had taken. Abraha, expecting a man who would beg for the protection of the Kaaba, was astonished.

*"I came to destroy your sacred house,"* Abraha said. *"And you ask about camels?"*

Abdul-Muttalib answered:

*"I am the lord of the camels. As for the House — it has a Lord of its own who will protect it."*

Then he returned to his people, held the ring of the Kaaba's door, and made dua:

*"O Allah, a man protects his home. Protect Your home. Let not their cross and their power overcome Your power."*

Then he released the ring and went to the mountains with his people.

He did not fight. He did not negotiate. He trusted. And what happened next, Arabia never forgot.

## A Man on the Edge of Light

Abdul-Muttalib was not a Muslim in the formal sense — he lived and died before the revelation came. He worshipped at the Kaaba alongside the idols of his people. He was a man of his time and his world.

And yet.

He prohibited wine for himself throughout his life. He forbade certain practices that the revelation would later confirm as prohibited — he was the first Arab to legislate against marrying a stepmother, against burying daughters alive in his own family. He believed, with a certainty that no idol had given him, that the Kaaba had a Lord who would protect it. His dua at the door of the Kaaba — *O Allah* — was addressed not to Hubal or al-Lat or al-Uzza. It was addressed to the One.

Scholars have noted that Abdul-Muttalib seems to have carried within him remnants of the Hanifiyya — the way of Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, the pure Tawhid that certain Arabs preserved across the centuries of idol worship, never fully extinguished.

He was not a Prophet. He was not a believer in the formal sense. But he was a grandfather — a man in whose arms the infant Muhammad ﷺ would be placed, whose name would be spoken at one of the most pivotal moments of the Prophet's ﷺ life, and whose character was part of the preparation Allah made for the arrival of the final light.

The Kaaba had its guardian. And the guardian's son — spared from the knife ten times — was about to start his own story.
