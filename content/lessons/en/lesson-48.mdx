## Before the City

The Prophet ﷺ arrived at the outskirts of Medina on a Monday — the same day of the week he had been born, the same day the first revelation had come.

But he did not ride straight into Medina.

He stopped at Quba — a settlement of the Banu Amr ibn Awf clan, a cluster of homes and date palm gardens on the southern edge of the city. He had ridden through the desert for days. He had spent three nights in a cave. He had been pursued by a man with a sword and a hundred-camel reward on his mind.

He stopped. And he built.

## The Foundation

The land he chose for the mosque in Quba belonged to two young orphans. The Prophet ﷺ refused to take it without fair compensation — even here, even now, even after everything — the obligations of justice did not pause for the circumstance.

He laid out the qiblah. He set the first stones with his own hands.

The companions رضي الله عنهم arrived — those who had migrated before him, those of the local Ansar — and they joined him. Stones were carried. Foundation walls were raised. The building was simple, as the first houses of Allah in Islamic history tended to be — the simplicity of people who understood that the purpose of a mosque was not its architecture but its worship.

The Prophet ﷺ worked alongside his companions رضي الله عنهم with a completeness that they often remarked on. He did not supervise. He carried stones. When they tried to take the stone from his hands, he refused.

One of the Ansar — moved by the sight of the Prophet ﷺ carrying building materials — recited a couplet:

*If we sit while the Prophet works — that would be a misguidance on our part.*

The others picked up the rhythm and joined in, carrying stones to the beat of the verse.

## What the Quran Said About It

Years later — after the hypocrites of Medina had built a rival mosque to cause division, and after that mosque had been exposed and condemned — the Quran described the contrast between the two:

<Quran surah="9" ayah="108" />

*Do not stand in it — ever. A mosque founded on taqwa from the first day is more worthy for you to stand in. Within it are men who love to purify themselves, and Allah loves those who purify themselves.*

The scholars of tafsir identified the mosque founded on taqwa from the first day as Masjid Quba.

The description carries layers of meaning. *From the first day* — not as an afterthought, not as a supplement to something else already built, but as the first act. The community was established on taqwa before it was established on anything else.

*Men who love to purify themselves* — the people of Quba had a particular reputation in the early Medinan community for their attention to ritual purity, specifically istinja. The Quran acknowledged this quality and connected it to Allah's love. A community whose attention to worship extended to the smallest details of purification was a community with the right foundation.

## The Prophet ﷺ Never Forgot It

After the Prophet ﷺ entered Medina and built his mosque there, he did not leave Masjid Quba behind.

Every Saturday — or every week, the narrations vary slightly — he would go to Quba. Walking if he was nearby. Riding if the distance required it. Two rak'ahs in the mosque he had built with his own hands.

He told his companions:

> *"Whoever purifies himself in his house and then comes to the Masjid of Quba and prays in it — he will have the reward of Umrah."*
> — Sunan Ibn Majah, 1412

The reward of a complete Umrah — for two rak'ahs in a simple mosque at the edge of Medina. The scholars have reflected on why the reward is so great. One answer is the intention embedded in the act: a person who travels specifically to this mosque, specifically to pray in the place the Prophet ﷺ built with his own hands, is a person whose heart is oriented rightly — toward the sunnah, toward the example, toward the love of what the Prophet ﷺ loved.

## The Model of a Mosque

Masjid Quba was simple. It had no minaret. No decorations. No dome. It was a structure built by travelers who had recently crossed a desert, using local materials, in the space of days.

And yet it was described by Allah Himself as a mosque founded on taqwa.

The lesson is not that mosques should be simple — the Prophet ﷺ would later approve the beautification of mosques as the community grew. The lesson is about priority and intention.

The first mosque was built before there was a market. Before there was a formal governance structure. Before anything else in the new community was organized. The first act in the new land was worship. The foundation was taqwa.

Every Muslim community that has flourished across history has understood this sequence intuitively — you build the mosque first, and everything else around it. The mosque is not one institution among many. It is the center from which the others radiate.

Quba established this. Not as a rule. As an example. The Prophet ﷺ put down stones with his own hands before he rode into the city, and everything about the community he built in Medina grew from the same priority those stones represented.

Allah first. Everything else in its proper order after.
