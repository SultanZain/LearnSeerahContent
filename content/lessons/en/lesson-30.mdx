## A Reputation That Traveled

The title *Al-Amin* did not stay within the walls of Mecca.

Trade in the ancient world was built on reputation. Merchants who traveled the routes between Arabia, Syria, Yemen, and Persia carried their names ahead of them — men whose word was known were welcomed; men whose dealing was questionable found doors closed before they knocked.

Muhammad ﷺ had not yet made his second journey to Syria when his reputation reached a woman who listened carefully to what the market said about the men it dealt with.

Khadijah bint Khuwaylid had reasons to listen carefully. She was a widow — twice over — managing a trading enterprise that her late husbands had left her and that she had expanded through her own sound judgment. She hired men to take her goods on the seasonal caravans. She had learned, through experience, how to read a man's reputation before she trusted him with her property.

What she heard about Muhammad ibn Abdullah was consistent, detailed, and came from people who had no particular reason to be generous in their assessments. He was honest. He was gentle with the men who worked under him. He was reliable to the letter of any agreement he made.

She sent for him.

## The Journey South and North

Khadijah offered Muhammad ﷺ terms more generous than she offered other men — she trusted his reputation enough to pay better for his honesty. He accepted, and set out for Syria with her servant Maysarah accompanying him.

Maysarah was watching. Not with suspicion — but with the attentiveness of a trusted servant carrying out his mistress's assessment.

What he saw on the journey confirmed everything Khadijah had heard. The young merchant's honesty in the market. His fairness with the men of the caravan. His patience when things went wrong and his reliability when they went right.

And he saw two other things that he could not entirely explain.

Throughout the journey, a cloud had moved with them — or more specifically, had moved with Muhammad ﷺ, providing shade over him alone while the rest of the caravan moved in the open sun.

And at a resting point near a monastery, a monk named Nestora had seen Muhammad ﷺ sit beneath a tree to rest. He came to Maysarah and asked: *Who is the man resting under that tree?*

Maysarah described him.

Nestora said: *No one has rested under that tree except a Prophet.*

Maysarah brought all of this back to Khadijah — along with goods that had sold at a profit that exceeded her expectations.

## The Proposal

Khadijah رضي الله عنها was, by this point, a woman in her forties — the exact age varies by narration, with the most commonly cited figure being forty, though some scholars argue she was younger. She had been proposed to by men of standing in Quraysh and had declined them.

She called her friend Nafisa bint Munya.

Nafisa went to Muhammad ﷺ and asked him — carefully, testing the ground — whether he had considered marriage. He was twenty-five. It was unusual, by the norms of the time, for a man of his circumstances to still be unmarried.

He said yes — but expressed concern about his financial situation. He did not have much.

Nafisa said: *What if the financial matter were not a problem? What if you were proposed to by a woman of beauty, wealth, nobility, and character — would you accept?*

He asked: *Who?*

*Khadijah.*

He went to his uncle. Abu Talib went to Khadijah's family. The formal proposal was made. And Khadijah رضي الله عنها — the most sought-after woman in Mecca, who had declined men of greater wealth and status — married the young man with the reputation and the honest eyes.

## Fifteen Years Before the Cave

They were married for fifteen years before the night on Hira.

Fifteen years of a partnership that the Prophet ﷺ would speak of for the rest of his life with a tenderness that never diminished, even long after her death. She bore him six children — two sons who died in infancy, and four daughters: Zaynab, Ruqayyah, Umm Kulthum, and Fatimah رضي الله عنهم — who grew up in a household of unusual warmth and dignity.

She supported him financially, making his freedom to contemplate and withdraw to Hira possible in a way it would not have been had he been burdened with the full weight of economic provision. She supported him emotionally — with a constancy and a depth of love that the narrations preserve in detail.

She knew him. This is the most important thing to understand about Khadijah رضي الله عنها. She did not know the Prophet ﷺ from a respectful distance. She knew him as a wife knows a husband — in the details and the texture of daily life, in the moments of ease and the moments of difficulty, in private and in public, across fifteen years of ordinary days.

## The Night He Came Trembling

When Muhammad ﷺ came home from the cave of Hira — shaking, asking her to cover him, saying *cover me, cover me* — and told her what had happened, Khadijah رضي الله عنها did not ask for time to think.

She said — immediately, without hesitation, with the certainty of a woman who had known this man for fifteen years:

*"By Allah, Allah will never disgrace you. You maintain family ties. You carry the burdens of others. You provide for those who cannot provide for themselves. You honor your guests. You help in times of genuine distress."*

Then she took him to her cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal — an elderly Christian scholar, blind with age, who had spent his life studying the ancient scriptures.

Muhammad ﷺ described what he had seen and heard. The dark cave. The figure that had come. The words: *Iqra* — Read.

Waraqah's response was immediate:

*"This is the Namus — the same one Allah sent to Musa. I wish I were young. I wish I could live to see the day when your people drive you out."*

*"Will they drive me out?"*

*"Yes. No man has ever come with what you have brought without being met with enmity. If I live to see your day, I will support you with all my strength."*

He did not live to see it. He died not long after. But his words — the words of a scholar of the previous scriptures, recognizing from within his own tradition what Muhammad ﷺ had just encountered — were preserved. They are one of the first external confirmations of the revelation, from the tradition that the revelation had come to complete.

## The First Believer

Khadijah رضي الله عنها was the first person to accept Islam.

Before Abu Bakr رضي الله عنه. Before Ali رضي الله عنه. Before any of the companions رضي الله عنهم whose names history has made famous. The first human being to hear the Prophet's ﷺ claim and say *I believe* — was his wife.

The woman who knew him best. The woman with fifteen years of unobstructed access to the private person behind the public reputation. The woman who had watched him in grief and in ease, in confidence and in vulnerability.

Her belief was not the belief of a stranger persuaded by a speech. It was the belief of a witness — someone who had seen enough of the man to know that what he was saying about himself was consistent with everything she had ever known him to be.

The Prophet ﷺ never forgot her. Years after her death, when he was the leader of a growing community in Medina, surrounded by younger wives and new companions, he would still speak her name with a love that was unmistakable.

When Aisha رضي الله عنها once expressed some jealousy at how often he spoke of Khadijah, he answered:

> *"She believed in me when people disbelieved. She trusted me when people rejected me. She supported me with her wealth when people abandoned me. And Allah gave me children through her."*
> — Musnad Ahmad, 24864

She was, in the fullest sense of the word, his partner — in life, in hardship, in the mission that the night on Hira had begun.

The crucible of character was complete. The years of shepherding, the desert solitude, the journey to Syria, the pact of the virtuous, the fifteen years of partnership with the finest woman in Arabia — all of it had formed a man ready to carry what the cave of Hira was about to give him.

The mission was beginning. And it would begin, as all great things do, not with armies or proclamations — but with a single word in the dark.

*Iqra.*

*Read.*
