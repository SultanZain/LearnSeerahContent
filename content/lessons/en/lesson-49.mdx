## The Friday of Arrival

He had been at Quba for several days when he prepared to ride into Medina proper.

It was a Friday morning. The timing was not incidental — Friday carried its own significance in the emerging rhythm of the community, and the Prophet ﷺ was about to give that significance its permanent form.

He mounted Qaswa, his she-camel. The companions who had gathered at Quba rode with him. And as they moved northward into the city, something remarkable was happening on both sides of the road.

People were coming out of their homes.

## The Valley of Ranuna

On the way from Quba to Medina, the road passed through the valley of Ranuna — where the tribe of Banu Salim ibn Awf had their settlement and a place of prayer.

The Prophet ﷺ stopped.

He led the Friday prayer — the first Jumu'ah in Islamic history, conducted not in a grand mosque but in an open space in a valley, with the companions who had accompanied him and the people of Banu Salim who came to join.

He delivered a khutbah. The early sources preserve its opening:

*"Praise be to Allah — I praise Him and seek His help and forgiveness, and I seek His refuge from the evil of our souls. Whoever Allah guides, none can misguide him. Whoever He leads astray, none can guide him. I testify that there is no god but Allah alone with no partner, and that Muhammad is His servant and messenger."*

Then he spoke to the assembled community about taqwa, about the obligations of brotherhood, about the reality of death and the necessity of preparation.

The first Friday sermon. In a valley. With a community that had no permanent home yet.

## The Arrival

After the prayer, the Prophet ﷺ rode into Medina.

What happened next is one of the most beloved scenes in the entire Seerah — preserved not in the language of formal historical record but in the warmth of people who witnessed it and could not help transmitting the feeling alongside the facts.

The city came out.

Men stood along the roads. Women climbed to their rooftops to see. Children ran. The sound of the city — normally the sound of a busy agricultural settlement, date palms and commerce and the ordinary noise of community life — became something else.

The nasheed that is associated with this arrival — *Tala'a al-Badru 'alayna, min thaniyyat al-Wada'* — the full moon has risen over us from the valley of farewell — carries in its words the feeling of that day: the city was seeing something rise over it that would illuminate it forever.

Every family of the Ansar reached out as the camel passed. *Stay with us, O Messenger of Allah. Stay with us — we have what you need, we have protection, we have love.*

The Prophet ﷺ smiled at them. He loved them for it — he would say so, explicitly, for the rest of his life. But he told them: let the camel walk. She will stop where Allah wills.

## Where the Camel Knelt

Qaswa moved through the streets of Medina with the slow, deliberate pace of a camel that is not being directed. The crowds made way. The families watched.

She came to an open area near the homes of Banu al-Najjar — one of the clans of the Khazraj, the maternal uncles of Abdul-Muttalib, which meant they were the ancestral family of the Prophet ﷺ himself through his grandmother.

She knelt. Then she rose and walked a few steps. Then she turned and knelt again at the same spot.

The Prophet ﷺ said: *This is the abode, if Allah wills.*

He looked around and asked whose property this was. He was told it belonged to two young orphan brothers — Sahl and Suhayl, sons of Amr, in the care of As'ad ibn Zurara <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>. He arranged for the land to be purchased at fair value — the Prophet ﷺ would not take even the land his mosque would be built on as a gift, insisting on payment.

The nearest home to that land belonged to Abu Ayyub Khalid ibn Zayd al-Ansari <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>. The Prophet ﷺ stayed with him while the mosque was being built.

## Seven Months with Abu Ayyub

Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> and his wife were not the most prominent family in Medina. They were not the wealthiest or the most politically connected. They were the family at whose door the camel stopped.

They received their guest with a care that the narrations preserve in loving detail.

Their home had two floors. The Prophet ﷺ was given the lower floor — cooler, more accessible, appropriate for receiving visitors. Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> and his wife took the upper floor.

Then Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> realized something that kept him awake at night: they were sleeping above the Prophet ﷺ. Walking above the Prophet ﷺ. Their feet were above his head.

He went to the Prophet ﷺ and asked, with genuine distress, to swap floors. The Prophet ﷺ — preferring the lower floor for practical reasons — declined. Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> was so troubled that the narrations say he and his wife moved carefully to the edges of the upper room, afraid to walk in the center lest they be directly above the Prophet ﷺ.

One night a jar of honey broke on the upper floor. The liquid began to seep toward the edge. Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> and his wife spent the night soaking it up with their only blanket — afraid of it reaching the room below, afraid of causing the Prophet ﷺ any discomfort.

This is the texture of what the Ansar gave. Not the grand gesture of a pledge made at al-Aqabah in the dark — though they gave that too. The daily, ordinary, unglamorous texture of love expressed in a broken jar and a blanket sacrificed in the night.

The Prophet ﷺ stayed with Abu Ayyub <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> for approximately seven months — until the mosque and its adjacent quarters were complete. He never forgot the family that had received him when the camel stopped at their door.
