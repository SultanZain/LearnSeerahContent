## The Last Before the Last

Every chain has a final link before it connects to its end.

In the long chain of Prophethood sent to the Children of Israel — Ishaq, Yaqoob, Yusuf, Musa, Harun, Dawud, Sulayman, and many others <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the final link was the most extraordinary of all.

Isa ibn Maryam <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>. Born without a father. Given miracles that made the dead rise and the blind see. Raised to Allah without tasting death. And destined to return before the end of time.

His story is inseparable from his mother's — because without understanding Maryam <span className="font-ar">عليها السلام</span>, you cannot understand him.

## Maryam — Before the Son

Maryam <span className="font-ar">عليها السلام</span> was not an ordinary woman. The Quran dedicates an entire surah to her — the only surah named after a woman — and the Prophet ﷺ named her among the four greatest women in all of human history.

She had been devoted to the worship of Allah from before her birth. Her mother had dedicated her, while still in the womb, to the service of the temple. She grew up in a state of such constant worship that Zakariyya <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — the Prophet assigned as her guardian — would find fresh provisions in her chamber that he had not brought.

*O Maryam, where does this come from?*

<Quran surah="3" ayah="37" />

*It is from Allah. Allah provides for whom He wills without account.*

Then one day, an angel appeared to her in the form of a man. She recoiled immediately — *I seek refuge in Allah from you, if you fear Him.* The angel identified himself and told her: *I am only a messenger from your Lord, to give you a pure son.*

She said: *How can I have a son when no man has touched me?*

The angel answered:

<Quran surah="19" ayah="21" />

*Thus it will be. Your Lord says: it is easy for Me.*

## Born Under a Palm Tree

Maryam <span className="font-ar">عليها السلام</span> withdrew from her people — alone — and gave birth far from home, under a palm tree, in pain and isolation so complete that she cried out:

<Quran surah="19" ayah="23" />

*I wish I had died before this and was completely forgotten.*

That is the reality the Quran does not soften. The greatest woman in human history, carrying the most miraculous child ever born, and she was alone, in pain, overwhelmed. Allah did not remove the difficulty — He accompanied her through it.

A voice called out: *Do not grieve. Your Lord has placed a stream beneath you. Shake the trunk of the palm tree — fresh dates will fall. Eat, drink, and be comforted.*

And then: *If you see any person, say: I have vowed a fast of silence to the Most Merciful, and I will not speak today to any human.*

She returned to her people carrying the child. They erupted — the accusations of a woman who had spent her life in the temple, now returning with an infant and no husband. The shame and the fury poured over her.

She pointed to the child.

*How can we speak to one who is yet an infant in the cradle?*

And Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> spoke.

<Quran surah="19" ayah="30-33" />

*Indeed I am a servant of Allah. He has given me the scripture and made me a Prophet. He has made me blessed wherever I am, and has enjoined upon me prayer and charity as long as I live.*

A newborn. Announcing his own prophethood. Defending his mother's honor. Declaring his servitude to Allah — not his divinity, but his servitude — from the very first words he ever spoke.

## The Miracles

As Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> grew and his mission began, he was given miracles of a scale and intimacy with human suffering that no Prophet before him had received in the same way.

He formed birds from clay and breathed into them — and they flew, by Allah's permission. He cured the leper and the blind — not with medicine, but with a word. He brought the dead back to life.

<Quran surah="3" ayah="49" />

Each miracle was tailored to his people. The Children of Israel at that time were deeply impressed by medicine and the physical sciences. So Allah gave Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> the most dramatic possible demonstration that the power of life and death belongs to Allah alone — and that this man standing before them was connected to that power in a way no physician ever could be.

He also did something quieter and more unsettling for his audience: he told people what they had eaten and what they had stored in their homes.

*In that is a sign for you, if you are believers.*

## The Message He Carried

For all the extraordinary nature of his birth and his miracles, Isa's <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> message was the same as every Prophet before him. Not a new theology. Not a new God. The same truth, delivered again to a people who had drifted from it.

<Quran surah="5" ayah="72" />

*Allah is my Lord and your Lord — so worship Him. This is the straight path.*

He confirmed the Torah. He eased certain rulings that had been made strict for the Children of Israel as a consequence of their transgressions. He called them back to the pure core of Ibrahim's <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> religion — the Tawhid that all the laws and all the Prophets had always been built upon.

And he told them something else. Something they would later try to erase from their records.

<Quran surah="61" ayah="6" />

*And giving good tidings of a messenger to come after me whose name is Ahmad.*

Ahmad — another name of Muhammad ﷺ, from the same root of praise, *hamd*. The explicit announcement of the final Prophet, from the lips of his immediate predecessor. The handoff, stated clearly, preserved in the Quran even as it was obscured in the scriptures that followed.

## What They Did With His Message

Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> gathered disciples — the Hawariyyun, the companions who answered his call and supported him. A small group, faithful and committed, who said:

<Quran surah="3" ayah="52" />

*We are the helpers of Allah. We believe in Allah, and bear witness that we are Muslims.*

But the people at large — the religious establishment, those with power invested in the existing order — rejected him and sought to kill him.

Allah did not allow it.

<Quran surah="4" ayah="157-158" />

They did not crucify him. They did not kill him. What they killed was someone else — made to appear as Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> to those who pursued him. Allah raised Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> to Himself, alive, where he remains — and will return, the Prophet ﷺ told us, before the Day of Judgement.

He will descend near Damascus. He will pray behind the leader of the Muslims. He will live his natural life. He will die and be buried. And then — the end will come.

## After He Was Gone

In the absence of Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, what happened to his message followed the pattern we have seen before — only faster and more dramatic.

Within a few generations, councils of men gathered and debated the nature of Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>. Was he God? The son of God? Part of a trinity? The man who had stood in front of crowds and said *Allah is my Lord and your Lord — worship Him* was elevated, step by step, into divinity.

The scripture was altered. The announcement of Ahmad was buried. The theology of Tawhid that every Prophet had carried was replaced with a doctrine that Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> himself would not have recognized.

And the world waited. For the one who had been announced. For the final answer to the drift. For the Prophet whose scripture would be preserved and whose message would need no renewal because it would be the last.

Arabia was waiting. And in Mecca, in the tribe of Quraysh, in the lineage of Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, a child was about to be born.

## The Prophets We Carried With Us

Before we close this unit, there is something important to say.

We walked through this chain — Adam, Nuh, Ibrahim, Ismail, Ishaq, Musa, Dawud, Sulayman, and Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — because these are the Prophets whose stories the Quran tells in the greatest depth, and whose lives form the essential backdrop for understanding Muhammad ﷺ and the message he brought.

But the chain of Prophethood is far longer than what we covered here.

There was Idris <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, raised to a high station by Allah. Hud <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, sent to the mighty people of Aad who built towering structures in the valleys. Salih <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, who came to the Thamud with a she-camel as a living sign — and watched them slaughter it and seal their own fate. Lut <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, who called his people away from sins that no people before them had committed, and was saved with only his daughters as an entire city was overturned. Shuaib <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, the eloquent Prophet of Madyan, who called his merchants to honest weights and fair dealing. Yaqoob <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, Israel himself, father of twelve sons and a lifetime of patient grief. Yusuf <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, whose story the Quran calls the most beautiful ever told — from the well to the palace, from slavery to sovereignty, with forgiveness at the end of it all. Yunus <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, who left his people without permission, found himself in the belly of a whale in the depths of a dark sea, and called out to Allah in the most famous dua of distress ever recorded. Zakariyya and Yahya <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span>, father and son, both Prophets, both martyred.

Each of them lived a story worthy of a lifetime of study. What we offered here was a reflection — enough to understand the chain, enough to feel its weight, enough to appreciate what it built toward.

For the full and detailed account of each Prophet's life, we point you to one of the most beloved books in the Islamic tradition:

> **[Qisas al-Anbiya — Stories of the Prophets by Ibn Kathir](https://islamhouse.com/en/books/400/)** — a comprehensive account of the lives of all the Prophets, drawn from the Quran, the Sunnah, and the classical commentaries. Read it alongside what you learn here, and the picture will become complete.

The Prophets are not just history. They are guidance made human. And every one of them was pointing, in the end, toward the same destination — the man whose life we are now about to begin studying in full.

The chain is complete. The stage is set. In the next unit, we turn to the question of finality — what it means that Muhammad ﷺ is the last, and why that is not an ending but a completion.
