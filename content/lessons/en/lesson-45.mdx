## The Night Meeting

It was the middle of the night. The pilgrimage season was at its height — thousands of people from across Arabia camped in and around Mecca, their presence covering the movement of a small group of people who had come for a meeting that could not be announced.

Seventy-three men and two women of the Aws and Khazraj had made their way, quietly, to the pass of al-Aqabah. They had come to Mecca officially as pilgrims. What they were actually here for was a covenant that would change the history of the world.

The Prophet ﷺ came to them in the dark, accompanied by his uncle Abbas — not yet a Muslim, but a man who had spent his life protecting his nephew and was not about to stop now.

## Abbas Speaks First

Before the Prophet ﷺ said anything, Abbas stood and addressed the assembly of Yathribis.

His message was simple and direct — the message of a man who understood what was at stake and wanted no ambiguity:

*"O people of the Khazraj — you know the position that Muhammad holds among us. We have protected him from our own people as much as we could. He is honored and protected in his own land. But he has chosen to join you and to leave with you. If you think you can fulfill what you are calling him to and protect him against those who would harm him — then take him and the responsibility is yours. But if you think you might abandon him or hand him over after you have taken him — then leave him now. Because abandonment after protection is a greater dishonor than refusal from the start."*

The Yathribis heard this. They understood it. And they answered — speaking from the conviction of people who had spent a year with Musab ibn Umayr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>, who had seen their city transformed by the message, who had come five hundred kilometers for this night:

*"We have heard what you said. And now, O Messenger of Allah — speak. Take for yourself and for your Lord whatever you wish."*

## The Covenant

The Prophet ﷺ recited from the Quran. He described the obligations of the covenant. Then he stated what he was asking:

*"I take your pledge that you will protect me as you protect your women and your children."*

Al-Bara ibn Marur <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> — one of the chiefs of the Khazraj, an elder of the group — reached out and took the Prophet's ﷺ hand.

*"By Him who sent you with the truth — we will protect you as we protect our own families. Take our pledge, O Messenger of Allah. By Allah — we are the sons of war and the people of weapons, inherited from father to son."*

One by one, they came forward and pledged. Some took his hand. Others pledged by name. The Prophet ﷺ accepted each pledge.

The covenant was made.

The Yathribis had come as pilgrims. They were leaving as the Ansar — the Helpers — a title that would belong to them until the Day of Judgement.

## The Twelve Chieftains

After the general pledge, the Prophet ﷺ asked them to select twelve chieftains from among themselves — nine from the Khazraj and three from the Aws — who would be responsible for their people's affairs, as Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had selected chieftains from the Children of Israel.

The twelve were chosen. The Prophet ﷺ told them:

*"You are the guarantors of your people, as the disciples of Isa ibn Maryam were guarantors for their people. And I am the guarantor for my people."*

The structure of responsibility was clear. The migration that was coming was not a casual movement of people — it was an organized transfer of a community from one city to another, with formal accountability on both sides.

## Quraysh Discovers the Meeting

The meeting broke up in the darkness before dawn. The Yathribis slipped back to their camps in the pilgrimage grounds, resuming the appearance of ordinary pilgrims.

But news traveled in Arabia, and not always in the directions its bearers intended.

By morning, word had reached Quraysh that a meeting had taken place at al-Aqabah. They went to the Yathribi camps and confronted the pilgrims: *"We have heard that you came to our man at night to invite him to come to you, and that you have given him a pledge against us."*

The polytheists in the Yathribi delegation — who had come with the pilgrimage but were not part of the covenant — genuinely did not know what had happened and said so truthfully.

The Muslims in the delegation said nothing. Or said what protected the secret.

Quraysh sent out men to intercept the departing Yathribi pilgrims on the road. They caught Sa'd ibn Ubadah <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> — a chief of the Khazraj and one of the signatories of the covenant. He was brought back to Mecca and mistreated.

But two men — Jubayr ibn Mutim and al-Harith ibn Harb — had personal debts of honor to Sa'd. They intervened and secured his release.

He walked out of Mecca free, rejoined the people of Yathrib on the road, and left.

The window had closed.

## What the Pledges Built

The two pledges of Aqabah — separated by a year, escalating in their commitment — were the diplomatic and moral foundation of the Hijrah.

The first pledge established the faith. The second established the protection.

Together, they established something that had never existed before: a city that had formally invited the Prophet of Allah and pledged with their lives to receive him and defend his mission.

It was an extraordinary act of commitment by people who had everything to lose and nothing tangible to gain — except the truth they had recognized when Musab ibn Umayr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> had recited the Quran in their gardens, when the message had arrived in a city exhausted by its own violence and hungry for something that could make sense of the wreckage.

The Ansar would go on to do what they pledged. They would receive the muhajirin — the emigrants from Mecca — and share with them everything they had. They would stand beside the Prophet ﷺ in battle and in peace. They would give up the pride of ownership over the city they had built so that a community of strangers could call it home.

The Prophet ﷺ would never forget what they gave.

> *"Were it not for the Hijrah, I would be a man of the Ansar. If the people went one way and the Ansar went another — I would go with the Ansar."*
> — Sahih al-Bukhari, 3779

The city was ready. The covenant was made. All that remained was the departure.

And Quraysh — who had discovered too late what had been arranged in the darkness of al-Aqabah — were about to discover that the man they had tried for thirteen years to destroy had found a door they had not thought to guard.
