## The Question Nobody Asks

Most people who grow up with religion accept it as a given. Prophets existed. Messages came. We follow.

But rarely do we stop and ask: *why?* Why did Allah not simply place the truth in every human heart directly? Why did guidance require a human being to stand in the marketplace and call people to it?

This is not a trivial question. It is the foundation upon which the entire story of Prophethood rests.

## What the Human Mind Can — and Cannot — Do

The human mind is one of the greatest gifts Allah has placed in creation. With it, civilizations were built. With it, the stars were mapped and medicines were discovered. With it, a person sitting alone in the desert can look at the sky and reason his way to the conclusion that all of this did not simply happen by chance — that behind this universe is a Creator.

That much, the human mind can reach on its own. And it is no small thing.

But here is where the mind reaches its limit.

Once a person has concluded that a Creator exists — *then what?* How is this Creator to be worshipped? What does He want from us? What is truly just and what is truly wrong? What happens when we die? What are our obligations to one another?

These are not questions that any civilization, left to itself, has ever answered consistently or correctly. The ancient Greeks produced philosophers of extraordinary brilliance — and yet their gods committed adultery and treachery. The Romans built an empire of law — and yet burned their enemies alive as entertainment. Every society that constructed its morality from pure human reasoning alone eventually bent that reasoning to serve power, desire, or convenience.

The mind can point toward Allah. It cannot, on its own, show us the full path to Him.

## The Traveller Without a Map

Imagine a man who has been told that there is a city — a real city, with food, shelter, and safety — somewhere beyond the mountains. He believes it exists. He wants to reach it. He is intelligent, determined, and willing to walk.

But he has never been there. He has no map. And the terrain ahead is unknown.

He may walk for years. He may come close. He may even stumble upon paths that look right but lead into dead ends or off the edges of cliffs.

Now imagine someone hands him a map — drawn by someone who knows that land completely, who has walked every path and can say with certainty: *this way leads there, that way does not.*

That map is revelation. That guide is the Prophet.

The traveller is not foolish. His legs are strong and his desire is genuine. But no amount of intelligence replaces firsthand knowledge of the road.

## The Evidence of History

This is not theoretical. History makes the argument plainly.

Look at what happens to a people after their Prophet has passed and the message begins to fade. The Children of Israel received the Torah through Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> — a complete and luminous guidance. Within generations of his passing, they had constructed golden calves to worship. The followers of Isa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> received a clear message of Tawhid — and within centuries had elevated him to divinity.

This is not a condemnation of those peoples. It is a description of human nature. Without a living, renewed guidance, the truth gradually becomes distorted. Each generation inherits a slightly altered version of what came before, until the original is barely recognizable.

This is why Allah did not send one Prophet and leave humanity to manage for eternity. He sent, across the full span of human history, messenger after messenger — each renewing the same essential truth for his own people and his own time.

## Justice Requires a Warning

There is one more dimension to this that must be understood — and it is rooted in the justice of Allah.

Allah does not punish a people for whom no warning was sent. This is not a concession or a loophole. It is a direct expression of His perfect justice. A person who never received the message, who lived and died with no Prophet reaching them, is not held accountable for what they never knew.

But once a Prophet arrives — once the message is delivered clearly, in the language of the people, by someone whose life itself is evidence of his sincerity — then the argument is established. The choice becomes real. And accountability becomes just.

This is what scholars call *iqamat al-hujjah* — the establishment of the divine argument. And it is one of the deepest reasons for Prophethood.

Allah did not send Prophets to watch humanity fail. He sent them so that no soul could stand before Him on the Day of Judgement and say: *I was never told.*

## So Why Prophets?

Because the human mind, as brilliant as it is, was never designed to navigate the unseen alone.

Because justice demands that guidance reaches every people before accountability is applied.

Because history shows, with painful clarity, what happens when humanity is left to construct its own truth.

And because Allah, in His mercy, chose not to leave us without a map.

That is why Prophets were sent. That is why their message matters. And that is why understanding the chain of Prophethood — from the first man to the last messenger — is not a matter of history alone. It is a matter of knowing why you are here, and how you are meant to live.

In our next lesson, we will ask the question that often follows this one: *if Allah wanted to guide us, why did He not simply speak to each of us directly?*
