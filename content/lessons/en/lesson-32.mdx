## The Last Ten Nights

He was in the cave as he had been for weeks. The month of Ramadan was in its last ten nights — the nights that, as the revelation would later teach, contain within them a night better than a thousand months.

He did not know that yet. He knew only the silence of the cave, the darkness outside, and the searching that had occupied him for years without resolution.

Then something came into the cave.

## The Encounter

The narration of what happened next is one of the most precisely transmitted accounts in all of Islamic history. Aisha رضي الله عنها narrated it from the Prophet ﷺ himself, and it reached us through a chain that scholars have verified with the highest degree of confidence.

Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> came to him.

Not as a gentle whisper. Not as a calm vision. He seized him — a word the Arabic uses is *ghatta*, an embrace of overwhelming force — and squeezed until Muhammad ﷺ felt he had reached the limit of what his body could endure.

Then he released him and said: *Iqra* — Read.

*"I cannot read,"* Muhammad ﷺ said. He was not deflecting. He was stating a fact. He had never been taught to read.

Again the embrace. Again the overwhelming pressure. Again the release.

*"Iqra."*

*"I cannot read."*

A third time. And on the third release, the words came — not a command to read what was written, but a revelation to recite what was being given:

<Quran surah="96" ayah="1-5" />

*Read in the name of your Lord who created. Created man from a clinging substance. Read, and your Lord is the Most Generous — who taught by the pen. Taught man what he did not know.*

Five verses. The first words of the Quran to descend to the earth. And they descended not as a gentle gift but as an event — physical, overwhelming, unmistakable.

## Running Down the Mountain

Muhammad ﷺ left the cave.

The narrations describe him descending the mountain in a state unlike anything he had experienced before — trembling, his heart pounding, his mind trying to process what had just happened to him.

One fear above all others gripped him as he descended: *have I lost my mind? Have I been possessed?*

The pre-Islamic Arabian world had a category for a man who spoke words that seemed to come from beyond himself — the *majnun*, the possessed, the one through whom jinn spoke. This was the category that Mecca would try to place him in. And in the first terrifying moments after the cave, Muhammad ﷺ was afraid it might be true.

He reached the bottom of the mountain. He reached his home. He went to Khadijah رضي الله عنها.

*"Cover me. Cover me."*

She covered him. She held him. She waited while the trembling subsided.

## The First Words After Revelation

When he was calm enough to speak, he told her everything. The cave. The figure. The embrace that had felt like it would crush him. The words that had come.

And then he said what he feared: *I am afraid of what has happened to me.*

Khadijah رضي الله عنها listened. She looked at the man she had known for fifteen years — in joy and in grief, in public and in the privacy of their home. She considered everything she knew about him.

Then she spoke. And her words, transmitted across fourteen centuries, remain one of the most beautiful pieces of testimony in the entire Seerah:

*"By Allah, Allah will never disgrace you. You maintain family ties. You carry the burdens of the weak. You provide for those who cannot provide for themselves. You honor your guests. You help in every genuine distress."*

She was not quoting scripture. There was no scripture yet. She was testifying — from direct, personal, fifteen-year knowledge — that the man sitting before her was not the kind of man to whom evil things happen. That his character was a protection. That the Lord who had always guided him would not abandon him to delusion now.

Then she took him to Waraqah.

## What Waraqah Confirmed

Waraqah ibn Nawfal — elderly, blind, a Christian scholar who had spent his life studying the ancient texts — listened as Muhammad ﷺ described what had happened.

When Muhammad ﷺ finished, Waraqah said:

*"This is the Namus — the same one who came to Musa. I wish I were young. I wish I could live to see the day when your people drive you out."*

*"Will they drive me out?"*

Waraqah's answer was steady and without softening:

*"Yes. No man has ever come with what you have brought without being met with enmity. If I live to see your day, I will support you with all my strength."*

He did not live long enough. But his recognition — from within the tradition of the People of the Book, from a man who had studied the descriptions of the final Prophet in the ancient texts — was the first external confirmation of what had happened in the cave.

The Namus. The same angel. The same divine channel that had connected heaven to earth through Musa <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and every Prophet in the chain — had opened again. In a cave above Mecca. To a forty-year-old man who could not read.

## The Pause — Al-Fatrah

After the first revelation, the revelation stopped.

For a period — scholars debate its exact length, but it was significant — no new verses came. Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> did not return. The cave was silent again.

This was its own kind of trial. The encounter on the mountain had been terrifying — but it had been real, unmistakable, beyond doubt. The silence that followed allowed doubt to creep back in. Had it been real? Would it come again?

Muhammad ﷺ went back to the mountain. He looked toward the horizon. He waited.

Then, one day, he saw Jibril <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> again — sitting on a throne between the sky and the earth, his true form filling the horizon. Muhammad ﷺ returned home trembling, and the next revelation descended:

<Quran surah="74" ayah="1-5" />

*O you who covers himself — arise and warn. And your Lord glorify. And your garments purify. And uncleanliness avoid.*

The pause was over. The mission had begun. The man who had come trembling down a mountain with five verses was now commanded to *arise and warn*.

Forty years of preparation. One night of revelation. And now — a lifetime of mission.

The city below the mountain did not know yet what had happened on it. But it was about to find out.
