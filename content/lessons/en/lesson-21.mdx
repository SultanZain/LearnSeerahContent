## Before the Light, the Dark

Every story of illumination begins with an honest account of the darkness it replaced.

The Seerah does not ask us to pretend that the Arabia of the 6th century was a noble civilization temporarily confused. It asks us to see clearly — to understand what the Prophet ﷺ was born into, what he was sent to transform, and what it cost him and his companions رضي الله عنهم to accomplish that transformation.

The word Islam uses for this period is *Jahiliyyah* — the Age of Ignorance. Not ignorance of poetry or commerce or genealogy — the Arabs excelled at all three. Ignorance of divine guidance. And in that void, human nature, left entirely to itself, had built something that carried both remarkable beauty and devastating cruelty, often in the same hand.

## The Tribal World

Arabia in the 6th century was not a nation. It was not an empire. It was a constellation of tribes — hundreds of them — each with its own territory, its own alliances, its own vendettas, and its own pride.

The tribe was everything. A man's identity, his protection, his law, and his meaning — all of it came from his tribe. Outside the tribe, a man was nobody. A man whose tribe was destroyed had nothing — no rights, no safety, no future.

This tribal loyalty produced real virtues. The protection of the guest was sacred — a man who sought refuge in a tribe's territory was untouchable, even if he was your enemy. The generosity of the tribal chief was a matter of honor — a chief who hoarded while his people went hungry was despised. Courage in battle was admired above almost everything else.

But the same tribalism produced a darkness that the most eloquent poet could not beautify.

Blood feuds. A single killing could ignite a war between tribes that lasted decades, consuming generation after generation in retaliatory violence, each side unable to stop without losing face. The famous War of Basus lasted forty years over a single camel.

And at the bottom of the tribal hierarchy — women, slaves, the poor, the orphan — existed with no rights, no recourse, and no protection beyond the goodwill of whoever held power over them.

## The Crime That Arabia Accepted

Of all the practices of pre-Islamic Arabia, one stands in its own category of horror.

*Wa'd al-banat.* The burying alive of infant daughters.

A man who feared poverty, or who felt shame at having a daughter rather than a son, would take the newborn girl — sometimes still warm from birth — and bury her in the earth. Alive.

The Quran addresses this directly, not with theological argument but with the most devastating image possible — the scene of judgement:

<Quran surah="81" ayah="8-9" />

*And when the girl buried alive is asked — for what sin was she killed?*

The question is asked not of the father, but of the daughter — because she is the one owed an answer. The accusation is in the asking. Allah does not argue against this practice. He simply asks, on the Day of Judgement, what she did to deserve it.

No father who had done this could answer.

The Prophet ﷺ came into a world where this was not a scandal. It was accepted. Practiced. Sometimes even spoken of with the language of family honor.

He would leave a world where it was unthinkable.

## Women in the Jahiliyyah

The situation of women in pre-Islamic Arabia extended beyond female infanticide into every dimension of life.

A woman had no right to inheritance. When her husband died, she could herself be inherited — passed to the man's son from another wife as though she were property. She had no right to choose her husband, no right to refuse a marriage, and in many cases no right to keep her children if her husband divorced her.

Divorce was entirely in the male domain — a man could divorce and take back his wife indefinitely, keeping her in a permanent state of suspension, neither fully married nor free.

The Quran would address each of these injustices specifically, directly, and with a comprehensiveness that transformed the status of women in a way the ancient world had never seen. But that transformation had to wait for a man willing to stand in the middle of Mecca and declare that the entire structure was wrong.

## The Kaaba, Filled With Idols

Perhaps the deepest wound of all was what had happened to the Kaaba.

Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> and Ismail <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had built it with their own hands for the exclusive worship of Allah — and their dua at its construction had asked that this place remain a sanctuary of pure Tawhid forever.

By the time the Prophet ﷺ was born, the Kaaba contained 360 idols. One for each day of the Arab lunar year, representing the gods of every major tribe. Hubal — the great idol brought from Syria — was positioned at the center. Al-Lat, al-Uzza, and Manat — the three daughters the Arabs had attributed to Allah — had their own shrines in and around Mecca.

The pilgrimage — the Hajj that Ibrahim <span className="font-ar">عليه السلام</span> had instituted as a call to the pure worship of Allah — had been distorted beyond recognition. Tribes had introduced their own rituals. Some groups performed the tawaf around the Kaaba entirely naked, on the rationale that they could not approach the sacred house wearing garments tainted by worldly sin. Merchants set up their stalls. Poets competed for prestige. The sacred months — during which all warfare was prohibited — were sometimes manipulated and rearranged for political convenience.

The house of Allah had become a carnival. And the God whose house it was had not been worshipped there in genuine Tawhid for centuries.

## What Was Still Good

And yet — and this matters — the Jahiliyyah was not a wasteland of pure evil.

The Arabs who would receive the Prophet ﷺ were not empty vessels. They had qualities that, once directed rightly, would make them the most extraordinary generation of believers the world had ever seen.

Their generosity was real — the tradition of feeding guests and the poor was deeply embedded in their culture. The Prophet ﷺ would not discard this quality; he would direct it toward Allah.

Their courage was extraordinary — men who had grown up in the harsh conditions of the desert, who settled disputes with the sword and rode into battle without hesitation. That courage, once turned toward the defense of truth, would carry Islam from the Arabian Peninsula to the edges of the known world within a generation.

Their loyalty to their word was absolute — an Arab who gave his oath and broke it was the lowest of men in his society's eyes. The companions رضي الله عنهم who pledged their lives to the Prophet ﷺ were people for whom a pledge was sacred.

And their language. Their extraordinary, precise, layered, beautiful language — capable of capturing realities that other tongues struggled to approach. A people whose language was their greatest pride were exactly the people needed to carry a revelation whose own language was its primary miracle.

The Jahiliyyah was darkness. But it was darkness filled with potential — raw, powerful, and ready to be ignited.

In our next lesson, that ignition begins — not with the Prophet ﷺ himself, but with an army that marched on Mecca, and the divine response that announced to all of Arabia that the hour was near.
