## The Emergency Council

The Muslims had been leaving Mecca in small groups — quietly, without announcing their departure, slipping away in the nights and early mornings, making their way north toward Yathrib.

Quraysh watched this with growing alarm. The community they had been persecuting for thirteen years — the people they had boycotted and tortured and mocked and tried to negotiate away — were leaving. Not fleeing in defeat, but migrating with purpose, to a city that had invited them and pledged to protect them.

If Muhammad ﷺ reached Yathrib, the calculation changed entirely. He would have the protection of two armed tribes, a city, a base. The man who could be persecuted in Mecca — because he had no tribal protection — could not be persecuted in Yathrib.

They had to stop him before he left.

The leaders of Quraysh gathered in the Dar al-Nadwa — the tribal council house, the building Qusayy had constructed for exactly these moments of collective decision. Every senior figure was present. Abu Jahl. Abu Sufyan. The men who had driven this opposition from the beginning.

An old man from the Najd — described in the Islamic tradition as Iblis in disguise — sat among them and contributed to the discussion.

Various plans were proposed: imprison him, exile him. Each one was rejected. Imprisonment would make him a cause. Exile would free him to organize against them from outside.

Then Abu Jahl spoke. And his plan was accepted.

One young man from each clan. Strike together. The blood across every family. Banu Hashim could not fight all of Quraysh at once.

*And remember when the disbelievers plotted against you to imprison you, or kill you, or drive you out. They plotted, and Allah plotted — and Allah is the best of plotters.*

## The Night They Surrounded His House

Armed young men — one from each clan of Quraysh, as the plan required — gathered outside the Prophet's ﷺ house in the darkness. They arranged themselves around every exit. They would wait for dawn, for the moment when they could see clearly, and then they would strike together.

Inside, the Prophet ﷺ had been informed of the plan — through revelation, through the divine knowledge that had always accompanied him.

He called Ali <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>. He told him what was happening. He told him to sleep in his bed, wrapped in the green Hadhrami cloak he himself normally wore. He told him to stay behind and return the trusts — the valuables that people across Mecca, including his enemies, had deposited with Al-Amin, knowing their property was safe with him.

Ali <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> lay down in the bed without question.

The Prophet ﷺ took a handful of dust. He recited:

<Quran surah="36" ayah="9" />

*And We have put before them a barrier and behind them a barrier and covered them, so they do not see.*

He walked out of his house. He walked through the armed men surrounding it, scattering dust on their heads as he passed.

None of them perceived him.

They found themselves — men posted at every exit, every window, every possible point of departure — covered in dust, but with no sense of how it had gotten there. The man they were waiting to kill had walked through them.

## To Abu Bakr's House and the Cave

The Prophet ﷺ went to Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span>.

This was the journey Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> had been preparing for — he had kept two camels ready and fed for months, knowing the day was coming. He wept when the Prophet ﷺ arrived — tears of joy, finally, after years of waiting.

They left Mecca in the darkness, taking a southern route — away from the road to Yathrib, deliberately counterintuitive, heading south before turning north. They went to a cave called Thawr, in the mountains south of Mecca.

They hid there for three days.

Abu Bakr's son Abdullah came to them each night with news of what Quraysh was saying and planning. His daughter Asma <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنها</span> — who would become famous for this act — brought them food, tearing her belt into two strips to tie the provisions, earning the title *Dhat al-Nitaqayn* — the Lady of the Two Belts.

Quraysh offered a reward of a hundred camels to anyone who brought them Muhammad ﷺ — dead or alive. Trackers went out in every direction. The trail was found and followed.

## The Trackers at the Cave

The trackers were skilled. They followed the trail south — the wrong direction, which puzzled them, but trails do not lie. They reached the foot of Jabal Thawr and began to climb.

They reached the entrance of the cave of Thawr.

Abu Bakr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> looked down from inside. He could see the feet of the trackers just outside the entrance. He whispered to the Prophet ﷺ — his voice barely above silence:

*"If one of them looks down at his feet, he will see us."*

The Prophet ﷺ answered with the words that the Quran would preserve:

<Quran surah="9" ayah="40" />

*"Do not grieve — indeed Allah is with us."*

The trackers stood at the cave entrance. They looked around. And then they turned back.

What they saw — or rather, what they did not see, and what they did see — was a spider's web across the entrance and a dove's nest settled at the mouth of the cave. Both had appeared overnight. The evidence of their own senses told them: no one has entered here recently. These were not disturbed.

They left.

Inside the cave, two men sat in the silence of the mountain. One had just told the other not to grieve. And outside, the Quraysh trackers walked away from the exact spot where the entire trajectory of human history was sitting in the dark.

## Three Days and Then the Road

After three days, when the immediate search had died down, the guide they had hired — Abdullah ibn Urayqit, a non-Muslim who was trusted for his knowledge of desert routes and his discretion — brought the camels.

They descended the mountain at night and headed north — not on the main road, where Quraysh's men might be watching, but on an unfamiliar coastal route that avoided the usual paths.

They were heading to Yathrib.

The city was waiting. Musab ibn Umayr <span className="font-ar">رضي الله عنه</span> had prepared the ground. The Ansar had pledged their lives. The second covenant of Aqabah had been sealed.

And the Prophet ﷺ — who had spent thirteen years in the city of his birth being mocked, tortured by proxy, offered everything he refused, besieged, stoned, and surrounded by armed men in the night — was riding north on a desert route toward a door that had been opened not by force or politics, but by the Quran recited in the gardens of date palms by a young man who had given up silk and wealth and family comfort for the truth.

Thirteen years. The mission had not been stopped.

It had been moving — quietly, steadily, through caves and night meetings and slave women who fed the community in a valley and a monk who wept at Surah Maryam and six men at al-Aqabah who said: we know who you are, we have been waiting.

Mecca was behind him.

The world was ahead.
